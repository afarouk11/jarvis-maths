title: Number & Place Value — Revision Notes
---
## Place Value

The position of a digit determines its value. In $3\,452.6$ the $4$ means $400$ (four hundreds) and the $6$ means $\frac{6}{10}$ (six tenths). Columns run: thousands, hundreds, tens, units, then a decimal point, then tenths, hundredths, thousandths.

## Types of Number

- **Integers** — whole numbers, positive or negative, including zero.
- **Natural numbers** — the counting numbers $1, 2, 3, \dots$
- **Rational numbers** — any number that can be written as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$.
- **Prime numbers** — exactly two factors ($1$ and itself). $2$ is the only even prime.
- **Square numbers** — $1, 4, 9, 16, 25, \dots$
- **Cube numbers** — $1, 8, 27, 64, \dots$
- **Triangular numbers** — $1, 3, 6, 10, 15, \dots$

## Factors, Multiples, Primes

- A **factor** divides exactly into a number.
- A **multiple** is in that number's times table.
- The **HCF** (Highest Common Factor) is the largest factor shared by two numbers.
- The **LCM** (Lowest Common Multiple) is the smallest multiple shared by two numbers.
- Use **prime factorisation** (factor trees) and **Venn diagrams** to find HCF and LCM reliably.

## Ordering & Rounding

- Compare decimals by aligning place value columns, working left to right.
- **Rounding to decimal places (d.p.):** look at the next digit; $5$ or more rounds up.
- **Significant figures (s.f.):** the first significant figure is the first non-zero digit. Keep place value with zeros.

## Estimation & Bounds

- Estimate by rounding each value to $1$ s.f. then calculating.
- **Error bounds:** a value rounded to the nearest $10$ that gives $80$ lies in $75 \le x < 85$. The lower bound is included; the upper bound is not.

## Operations

Use the order of operations (**BIDMAS**): Brackets, Indices, Division/Multiplication, Addition/Subtraction. Negative number rules: two like signs make a plus, two unlike signs make a minus.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Number & Place Value — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **BIDMAS order:** Brackets → Indices → Division/Multiplication → Addition/Subtraction.
- **HCF × LCM** $= $ product of the two numbers (for two numbers $a$ and $b$): $\text{HCF}(a,b) \times \text{LCM}(a,b) = a \times b$.
- **Rounding to 1 s.f.** for estimation: replace each number with its single-significant-figure approximation.
- **Bounds:** if $x$ is rounded to the nearest unit $u$ giving value $V$, then $V - \tfrac{u}{2} \le x < V + \tfrac{u}{2}$.
- **Negative rules:** $(+)(+) = +$, $(-)(-) = +$, $(+)(-) = -$, $(-)(+) = -$.
- $2$ is the only even prime number; $1$ is **not** prime.
- Significant figures: first non-zero digit counts first; trailing/place-holding zeros maintain magnitude.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Finding HCF and LCM by prime factorisation
---
Find the HCF and LCM of $72$ and $120$.

**Step 1 — prime factorise each number.**

$72 = 2^3 \times 3^2$ (since $72 = 8 \times 9$).

$120 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5$ (since $120 = 8 \times 15$).

**Step 2 — HCF: take the lowest power of each shared prime.**

Shared primes: $2$ and $3$. Lowest powers: $2^3$ and $3^1$.

$\text{HCF} = 2^3 \times 3 = 8 \times 3 = 24$.

**Step 3 — LCM: take the highest power of every prime appearing.**

$\text{LCM} = 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5 = 8 \times 9 \times 5 = 360$.

**Step 4 — check using HCF × LCM = product.**

$24 \times 360 = 8640$ and $72 \times 120 = 8640$. ✓

**HCF = 24 and LCM = 360.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Calculating with error bounds
---
A rectangle has length $l = 12$ cm and width $w = 8$ cm, each measured to the nearest centimetre. Find the upper bound for the area.

**Step 1 — write the bounds for each measurement.**

Nearest cm means $\pm 0.5$ cm.

$11.5 \le l < 12.5$ and $7.5 \le w < 8.5$.

**Step 2 — choose values for the maximum area.**

Area is largest when both length and width are largest, so use the upper bounds:

$l = 12.5$ cm, $w = 8.5$ cm.

**Step 3 — calculate.**

$\text{Area}_{\max} = 12.5 \times 8.5 = 106.25 \ \text{cm}^2$.

**The upper bound for the area is $106.25 \ \text{cm}^2$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Number & Place Value — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- When estimating, always round to **1 significant figure** unless told otherwise, and show the rounded calculation.
- For HCF take the **lowest** powers; for LCM take the **highest** powers — students frequently swap these.
- The upper bound is the value you would round **down** from, so it is written with $<$, not $\le$.
- Remember $1$ is not prime and $2$ is prime — a common trap.
- In BIDMAS, division and multiplication rank equally; work left to right. Same for addition and subtraction.
- Watch place-holding zeros when rounding to significant figures: $39\,600$ to $1$ s.f. is $40\,000$, not $4$.
- Negative number errors are extremely common — write the sign rule next to your working.

---
## What a Fraction Means

A fraction $\frac{a}{b}$ has a **numerator** $a$ (top) and a **denominator** $b$ (bottom). It represents $a$ parts out of $b$ equal parts, or the division $a \div b$.

## Equivalent Fractions & Simplifying

Multiply or divide numerator and denominator by the **same** number to make equivalent fractions. **Simplify** (cancel) by dividing both by their HCF, e.g. $\frac{12}{18} = \frac{2}{3}$.

## Improper Fractions & Mixed Numbers

- A **mixed number** like $2\tfrac{3}{4}$ combines a whole and a fraction.
- An **improper (top-heavy) fraction** like $\frac{11}{4}$ has numerator larger than denominator.
- Convert mixed → improper: $2\tfrac{3}{4} = \frac{2 \times 4 + 3}{4} = \frac{11}{4}$.

## Adding & Subtracting

You need a **common denominator** (use the LCM of the denominators). Convert each fraction, then add or subtract the numerators only. Keep mixed numbers separate or convert to improper first.

## Multiplying & Dividing

- **Multiply:** multiply the numerators and multiply the denominators, then simplify. Cancel before multiplying where possible.
- **Divide:** **K**eep, **C**hange, **F**lip — keep the first fraction, change $\div$ to $\times$, flip (reciprocal) the second.

$$\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c}$$

## Fractions of Amounts

To find $\frac{3}{5}$ of $40$: divide by the denominator, multiply by the numerator: $40 \div 5 \times 3 = 24$.

## Recurring Decimals

Every recurring decimal is a fraction. Use algebra: let $x$ equal the decimal, multiply by a power of $10$ to shift the recurring block, subtract, then solve.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Fractions — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Add/subtract:** $\dfrac{a}{b} \pm \dfrac{c}{d} = \dfrac{ad \pm cb}{bd}$ (then simplify).
- **Multiply:** $\dfrac{a}{b} \times \dfrac{c}{d} = \dfrac{ac}{bd}$.
- **Divide (KCF):** $\dfrac{a}{b} \div \dfrac{c}{d} = \dfrac{a}{b} \times \dfrac{d}{c} = \dfrac{ad}{bc}$.
- **Mixed → improper:** $w\tfrac{a}{b} = \dfrac{wb + a}{b}$.
- **Fraction of an amount:** $\dfrac{a}{b}$ of $N = N \div b \times a$.
- **Reciprocal** of $\dfrac{a}{b}$ is $\dfrac{b}{a}$ (and of $n$ is $\dfrac{1}{n}$).
- Always **simplify** the final answer by dividing top and bottom by their HCF.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Adding mixed numbers
---
Work out $2\tfrac{3}{4} + 1\tfrac{5}{6}$, giving your answer as a mixed number.

**Step 1 — convert to improper fractions.**

$2\tfrac{3}{4} = \frac{2 \times 4 + 3}{4} = \frac{11}{4}$ and $1\tfrac{5}{6} = \frac{1 \times 6 + 5}{6} = \frac{11}{6}$.

**Step 2 — find a common denominator.**

LCM of $4$ and $6$ is $12$.

$\frac{11}{4} = \frac{33}{12}$ and $\frac{11}{6} = \frac{22}{12}$.

**Step 3 — add the numerators.**

$\frac{33}{12} + \frac{22}{12} = \frac{55}{12}$.

**Step 4 — convert back to a mixed number.**

$55 \div 12 = 4$ remainder $7$, so $\frac{55}{12} = 4\tfrac{7}{12}$.

**The answer is $4\tfrac{7}{12}$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Converting a recurring decimal to a fraction
---
Express $0.4\overline{27} = 0.4272727\dots$ as a fraction in its simplest form.

**Step 1 — let $x$ equal the decimal.**

$x = 0.4272727\dots$

**Step 2 — multiply to line up the recurring block ($27$ repeats).**

Multiply by $10$: $10x = 4.272727\dots$

Multiply by $1000$: $1000x = 427.272727\dots$

**Step 3 — subtract to remove the recurring tail.**

$1000x - 10x = 427.2727\dots - 4.2727\dots$

$990x = 423$.

**Step 4 — solve and simplify.**

$x = \frac{423}{990}$. Divide top and bottom by $9$: $\frac{47}{110}$.

**The answer is $\dfrac{47}{110}$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Fractions — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Never add denominators when adding fractions — only the numerators change once denominators match.
- Always **convert mixed numbers to improper fractions** before multiplying or dividing.
- For division, only flip the **second** fraction, not the first.
- Cancel before multiplying to keep numbers small and avoid errors.
- When converting recurring decimals, multiply by the power of $10$ that aligns the recurring blocks exactly before subtracting.
- Always give a final answer in **simplest form**, and as a mixed number if the question started with mixed numbers.
- "Of" means multiply.

title: Percentages — Revision Notes
---
## Percentage Basics

"Per cent" means "out of $100$". To convert: percentage → decimal divide by $100$ ($35\% = 0.35$); decimal → percentage multiply by $100$.

## Finding a Percentage of an Amount

Use a **multiplier**: $35\%$ of $80 = 0.35 \times 80 = 28$. Non-calculator, split into $10\%$, $5\%$, $1\%$ and combine.

## Percentage Change

$$\text{Percentage change} = \frac{\text{change}}{\text{original}} \times 100$$

This covers profit, loss, increase and decrease. Always divide by the **original** amount.

## Increase & Decrease by Multipliers

- Increase by $20\%$ → multiply by $1.20$.
- Decrease by $20\%$ → multiply by $0.80$.

The multiplier is $1 \pm \frac{\text{percentage}}{100}$.

## Reverse Percentages

When a price already **includes** a change, divide by the multiplier to find the original. If $\pounds 60$ is the price after a $20\%$ increase, original $= 60 \div 1.20 = \pounds 50$. Do **not** simply subtract $20\%$.

## Simple vs Compound Interest

- **Simple interest** adds the same amount each year (based on the original).
- **Compound interest** applies the multiplier repeatedly — interest on interest.

$$\text{Amount} = P \times (\text{multiplier})^n$$

where $P$ is the principal and $n$ the number of periods. The same formula models **depreciation** (use a multiplier less than $1$) and **growth/decay**.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Percentages — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Percentage of amount:** value $= \dfrac{p}{100} \times N$.
- **Percentage change:** $\dfrac{\text{new} - \text{original}}{\text{original}} \times 100$.
- **Increase multiplier:** $1 + \dfrac{p}{100}$. **Decrease multiplier:** $1 - \dfrac{p}{100}$.
- **Reverse percentage:** original $= \dfrac{\text{final value}}{\text{multiplier}}$.
- **Compound growth/interest:** $A = P\left(1 + \dfrac{r}{100}\right)^{n}$.
- **Depreciation/decay:** $A = P\left(1 - \dfrac{r}{100}\right)^{n}$.
- $10\% = \div 10$, $1\% = \div 100$, $50\% = \div 2$, $25\% = \div 4$ — handy non-calculator building blocks.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Reverse percentage problem
---
A laptop is reduced by $15\%$ in a sale and now costs $\pounds 612$. Find the original price.

**Step 1 — identify the multiplier for the change.**

A $15\%$ decrease means multiply by $1 - 0.15 = 0.85$.

**Step 2 — set up the relationship.**

$\text{original} \times 0.85 = 612$.

**Step 3 — divide to reverse the operation.**

$\text{original} = \frac{612}{0.85} = 720$.

**Step 4 — check.**

$720 \times 0.85 = 612$. ✓

**The original price was $\pounds 720$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Compound interest over several years
---
$\pounds 2500$ is invested at $3\%$ compound interest per year. Find the value after $4$ years, to the nearest penny.

**Step 1 — write the compound interest formula.**

$A = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{100}\right)^n$ with $P = 2500$, $r = 3$, $n = 4$.

**Step 2 — find the multiplier.**

$1 + \frac{3}{100} = 1.03$.

**Step 3 — substitute and evaluate.**

$A = 2500 \times 1.03^4$.

$1.03^4 = 1.12550881$.

$A = 2500 \times 1.12550881 = 2813.77\ (\text{to nearest penny})$.

**The investment is worth $\pounds 2813.77$ after $4$ years.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Percentages — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- For reverse percentages, **never** just subtract the percentage from the final amount — divide by the multiplier.
- Compound and simple interest give different answers; read the question carefully for which is required.
- In percentage change, always divide by the **original** value, not the new one.
- Learn the multiplier method — it is faster and reduces errors than working out the percentage then adding.
- Round money answers to $2$ decimal places (the nearest penny) unless told otherwise.
- For "percentage profit", the original is the cost price, not the selling price.
- A $20\%$ rise then a $20\%$ fall does **not** return to the start — multipliers $1.2 \times 0.8 = 0.96$.

===NOTE===
type: concept
title: Ratio & Proportion — Revision Notes
---
## What a Ratio Is

A ratio $a : b$ compares quantities. Order matters. Ratios **simplify** like fractions by dividing all parts by their HCF, e.g. $12 : 18 = 2 : 3$.

## Equivalent Ratios & the Unitary Method

Scale ratios by multiplying or dividing every part by the same number. The **unitary method** finds the value of one part first, then scales.

## Sharing in a Ratio

To share an amount in ratio $a : b : c$:

- Add the parts to find the total number of parts.
- Divide the amount by the total parts to find one part.
- Multiply each ratio number by the value of one part.

## Ratio as a Fraction & "1 : n" Form

In $3 : 5$, the first quantity is $\frac{3}{8}$ of the total. To write as $1 : n$, divide both parts by the first number.

## Direct & Inverse Proportion

- **Direct proportion** ($y \propto x$): as one increases, the other increases at the same rate. $y = kx$.
- **Inverse proportion** ($y \propto \frac{1}{x}$): as one increases, the other decreases. $y = \frac{k}{x}$.

Find the constant $k$ from given values, then use it. Other forms include $y \propto x^2$ and $y \propto \frac{1}{x^2}$.

## Best Buys & Recipes

Scale recipes by the unitary method. For best buys, compare **price per unit** (e.g. cost per $100$ g) and choose the cheaper rate.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Ratio & Proportion — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Sharing:** one part $= \dfrac{\text{total amount}}{\text{sum of ratio parts}}$, then multiply each part.
- **Fraction of total:** in $a : b$, first quantity $= \dfrac{a}{a+b}$ of the total.
- **Direct proportion:** $y = kx$, so $k = \dfrac{y}{x}$.
- **Inverse proportion:** $y = \dfrac{k}{x}$, so $k = xy$.
- **Square proportion:** $y = kx^2$; **inverse square:** $y = \dfrac{k}{x^2}$.
- **Unitary method:** find the value of $1$ unit, then multiply.
- **Best buy:** compare cost per fixed amount (e.g. per gram); smaller is better value.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Sharing in a ratio with a difference clue
---
Alex and Sam share money in the ratio $5 : 3$. Alex receives $\pounds 18$ more than Sam. How much does each receive?

**Step 1 — find the difference in parts.**

$5 - 3 = 2$ parts represent the $\pounds 18$ difference.

**Step 2 — find the value of one part.**

$1$ part $= \frac{18}{2} = \pounds 9$.

**Step 3 — multiply each share.**

Alex: $5 \times 9 = \pounds 45$.

Sam: $3 \times 9 = \pounds 27$.

**Step 4 — check the difference.**

$45 - 27 = 18$. ✓

**Alex receives $\pounds 45$ and Sam receives $\pounds 27$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Inverse proportion
---
$y$ is inversely proportional to $x$. When $x = 4$, $y = 15$. Find $y$ when $x = 10$.

**Step 1 — write the proportion equation.**

Inverse proportion means $y = \frac{k}{x}$.

**Step 2 — find the constant $k$.**

Substitute $x = 4$, $y = 15$: $15 = \frac{k}{4}$, so $k = 15 \times 4 = 60$.

**Step 3 — write the full rule.**

$y = \frac{60}{x}$.

**Step 4 — substitute $x = 10$.**

$y = \frac{60}{10} = 6$.

**When $x = 10$, $y = 6$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Ratio & Proportion — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Ratio order matters: $3 : 5$ is not the same as $5 : 3$. Match the order to the names in the question.
- For "difference" problems, divide the difference by the difference in **parts**, not by the total.
- Always find the value of one part (or one unit) first — this prevents most errors.
- For proportion, write the equation with $k$, find $k$, then substitute — don't try to do it in one jump.
- Inverse proportion: the constant is $k = xy$, so the product stays constant.
- For best buys, give a clear conclusion stating which item is better value and why.
- Don't forget to simplify final ratios.

---
## Index Laws

Indices (powers) follow these rules for the same base:

- **Multiplication:** $a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$.
- **Division:** $a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}$.
- **Power of a power:** $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$.
- **Zero power:** $a^0 = 1$ (for $a \ne 0$).
- **Negative power:** $a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}$ (reciprocal).
- **Fractional power:** $a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$ and $a^{m/n} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m$.

## Surds

A **surd** is an irrational root left in exact form, like $\sqrt{2}$. Surd rules:

- $\sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{b} = \sqrt{ab}$.
- $\dfrac{\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{b}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{a}{b}}$.
- $\sqrt{a^2 b} = a\sqrt{b}$ (simplifying).

## Simplifying Surds

Find the largest **square factor**. For example $\sqrt{50} = \sqrt{25 \times 2} = 5\sqrt{2}$.

## Adding & Subtracting Surds

Only combine **like** surds: $3\sqrt{2} + 4\sqrt{2} = 7\sqrt{2}$, but $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}$ cannot be simplified.

## Rationalising the Denominator

Remove surds from the bottom of a fraction. For $\frac{a}{\sqrt{b}}$, multiply top and bottom by $\sqrt{b}$. For $\frac{a}{c + \sqrt{b}}$, multiply by the **conjugate** $c - \sqrt{b}$ to use the difference of two squares.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Indices & Surds — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- $a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$
- $a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n}$
- $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$
- $a^0 = 1$, $a^1 = a$
- $a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}$
- $a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$, $\quad a^{m/n} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m = \sqrt[n]{a^m}$
- $\sqrt{a}\,\sqrt{b} = \sqrt{ab}$, $\quad \dfrac{\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{b}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{a}{b}}$
- **Rationalise:** $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{a}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{a}}{a}$; for $c \pm \sqrt{b}$ multiply by conjugate $c \mp \sqrt{b}$.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Evaluating a negative fractional index
---
Evaluate $\left(\dfrac{8}{27}\right)^{-2/3}$ as an exact fraction.

**Step 1 — deal with the negative power (take the reciprocal).**

$\left(\frac{8}{27}\right)^{-2/3} = \left(\frac{27}{8}\right)^{2/3}$.

**Step 2 — apply the cube root (denominator of the index).**

$\sqrt[3]{\frac{27}{8}} = \frac{3}{2}$.

**Step 3 — apply the square (numerator of the index).**

$\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{9}{4}$.

**The answer is $\dfrac{9}{4}$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Rationalising with a conjugate
---
Rationalise the denominator of $\dfrac{6}{4 + \sqrt{2}}$.

**Step 1 — multiply by the conjugate $4 - \sqrt{2}$.**

$\frac{6}{4 + \sqrt{2}} \times \frac{4 - \sqrt{2}}{4 - \sqrt{2}}$.

**Step 2 — expand the denominator (difference of two squares).**

$(4 + \sqrt{2})(4 - \sqrt{2}) = 16 - 2 = 14$.

**Step 3 — expand the numerator.**

$6(4 - \sqrt{2}) = 24 - 6\sqrt{2}$.

**Step 4 — write and simplify.**

$\frac{24 - 6\sqrt{2}}{14}$. Divide each term by $2$: $\frac{12 - 3\sqrt{2}}{7}$.

**The answer is $\dfrac{12 - 3\sqrt{2}}{7}$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Indices & Surds — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- A negative index means **reciprocal**, not a negative answer.
- For $a^{m/n}$, the denominator $n$ is the root and the numerator $m$ is the power; do the root first to keep numbers small.
- Only **like** surds can be added or subtracted — treat $\sqrt{2}$ like an algebraic term.
- $\sqrt{a} + \sqrt{b} \ne \sqrt{a+b}$ — a very common error.
- When simplifying surds, always look for the **largest** square factor to finish in one step.
- To rationalise a two-term denominator, multiply by its conjugate (change the middle sign).
- $a^0 = 1$ for any non-zero $a$, including fractions and negatives.

title: Standard Form — Revision Notes
---
## What Standard Form Is

Standard form (scientific notation) writes very large or very small numbers as:

$$A \times 10^n$$

where $1 \le A < 10$ and $n$ is an integer. $A$ must have exactly one non-zero digit before the decimal point.

- Large numbers have a **positive** power: $4\,500\,000 = 4.5 \times 10^6$.
- Small numbers have a **negative** power: $0.00032 = 3.2 \times 10^{-4}$.

## Converting To and From Standard Form

- **To standard form:** move the decimal point so one non-zero digit sits in front; the number of places moved is the power (left moves give positive powers for big numbers, negative for small numbers).
- **From standard form:** move the decimal point $n$ places — right for positive $n$, left for negative $n$.

## Calculating with Standard Form

- **Multiply/divide:** handle the number parts and the powers of $10$ separately using index laws, then adjust $A$ back into the range $1 \le A < 10$.

$$(a \times 10^m) \times (b \times 10^n) = (a \times b) \times 10^{m+n}$$

- **Add/subtract:** make the powers of $10$ the same first, then add or subtract the number parts.

## Using a Calculator

Use the $\times 10^x$ (or EXP / EE) button — never type "$\times 10$" with the caret, as this causes errors. Always check your answer is in proper standard form.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Standard Form — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Form:** $A \times 10^n$ with $1 \le A < 10$ and $n$ an integer.
- **Multiply:** $(a \times 10^m)(b \times 10^n) = ab \times 10^{m+n}$.
- **Divide:** $\dfrac{a \times 10^m}{b \times 10^n} = \dfrac{a}{b} \times 10^{m-n}$.
- **Add/subtract:** convert to the same power of $10$, then combine the number parts.
- Positive power $\Rightarrow$ large number; negative power $\Rightarrow$ small number (less than $1$).
- After any operation, **re-adjust** $A$ so that $1 \le A < 10$ (carry powers of $10$).
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Multiplying in standard form
---
Work out $(3.6 \times 10^5) \times (5 \times 10^{-8})$, giving your answer in standard form.

**Step 1 — multiply the number parts.**

$3.6 \times 5 = 18$.

**Step 2 — add the powers of 10.**

$10^5 \times 10^{-8} = 10^{5 + (-8)} = 10^{-3}$.

**Step 3 — combine.**

$18 \times 10^{-3}$.

**Step 4 — adjust into standard form ($1 \le A < 10$).**

$18 = 1.8 \times 10^1$, so $18 \times 10^{-3} = 1.8 \times 10^{1} \times 10^{-3} = 1.8 \times 10^{-2}$.

**The answer is $1.8 \times 10^{-2}$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Adding numbers in standard form
---
Work out $4.2 \times 10^6 + 7 \times 10^5$, giving your answer in standard form.

**Step 1 — make the powers of 10 equal.**

Rewrite $7 \times 10^5$ with power $10^6$: $7 \times 10^5 = 0.7 \times 10^6$.

**Step 2 — add the number parts.**

$4.2 \times 10^6 + 0.7 \times 10^6 = (4.2 + 0.7) \times 10^6 = 4.9 \times 10^6$.

**Step 3 — check standard form.**

$4.9$ is between $1$ and $10$, so no adjustment is needed.

**The answer is $4.9 \times 10^6$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Standard Form — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- $A$ must satisfy $1 \le A < 10$ — answers like $18 \times 10^{-3}$ are not yet in standard form.
- For small numbers, the power is **negative**; a common slip is forgetting the minus sign.
- When adding or subtracting, you must match the powers of $10$ first — you cannot just add the number parts.
- On a calculator, use the dedicated $\times 10^x$ button; typing it as $\times 10\,\hat{}\,n$ may misread.
- Count decimal-place moves carefully; an off-by-one error changes the power.
- After multiplying or dividing, always re-check and re-adjust $A$ back into range.
- Give the answer in the form requested (ordinary number vs standard form).

## Key Vocabulary

- A **term** is a single number, variable, or product (e.g. $3x^2$).
- An **expression** is terms joined by $+$ or $-$ (no equals sign).
- A **coefficient** is the number multiplying a variable.

## Collecting Like Terms

Add or subtract terms with **identical** variable parts: $5a + 3b - 2a = 3a + 3b$. You cannot combine $x$ and $x^2$.

## Expanding Brackets

- **Single bracket:** multiply every term inside by the term outside: $3(2x - 4) = 6x - 12$.
- **Double brackets (FOIL):** $(x + 3)(x + 5) = x^2 + 5x + 3x + 15 = x^2 + 8x + 15$.
- **Squaring a bracket:** $(x + a)^2 = x^2 + 2ax + a^2$.

## Factorising

- **Common factor:** take out the HCF: $6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3)$.
- **Quadratic** $x^2 + bx + c$: find two numbers that multiply to $c$ and add to $b$.
- **Difference of two squares:** $a^2 - b^2 = (a + b)(a - b)$.

## Index Laws in Algebra

$x^m \times x^n = x^{m+n}$, $\ x^m \div x^n = x^{m-n}$, $\ (x^m)^n = x^{mn}$.

## Algebraic Fractions

Simplify by factorising numerator and denominator and cancelling common factors. Add/subtract using a common denominator, exactly as with numeric fractions.

## Rearranging Formulae

To make a variable the subject, use inverse operations to isolate it, doing the same to both sides.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Algebraic Expressions — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Expand double brackets:** $(x+a)(x+b) = x^2 + (a+b)x + ab$.
- **Perfect square:** $(x+a)^2 = x^2 + 2ax + a^2$.
- **Difference of two squares:** $a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b)$.
- **Index laws:** $x^m x^n = x^{m+n}$; $\ x^m \div x^n = x^{m-n}$; $\ (x^m)^n = x^{mn}$; $\ x^0 = 1$.
- **Factorising $x^2 + bx + c$:** find $p, q$ with $pq = c$ and $p + q = b$, giving $(x+p)(x+q)$.
- **Algebraic fractions:** factorise fully, then cancel common factors top and bottom.
- Changing the subject: apply inverse operations equally to both sides.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Expanding and simplifying double brackets
---
Expand and simplify $(2x - 3)(x + 4)$.

**Step 1 — multiply each pair (FOIL).**

First: $2x \times x = 2x^2$.

Outer: $2x \times 4 = 8x$.

Inner: $-3 \times x = -3x$.

Last: $-3 \times 4 = -12$.

**Step 2 — write out all terms.**

$2x^2 + 8x - 3x - 12$.

**Step 3 — collect like terms.**

$8x - 3x = 5x$.

**The answer is $2x^2 + 5x - 12$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Simplifying an algebraic fraction
---
Simplify $\dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 + x - 6}$.

**Step 1 — factorise the numerator (difference of two squares).**

$x^2 - 9 = (x + 3)(x - 3)$.

**Step 2 — factorise the denominator.**

Two numbers multiplying to $-6$ and adding to $+1$ are $+3$ and $-2$.

$x^2 + x - 6 = (x + 3)(x - 2)$.

**Step 3 — write as a single fraction and cancel.**

$\frac{(x + 3)(x - 3)}{(x + 3)(x - 2)}$. Cancel the common factor $(x + 3)$.

**The answer is $\dfrac{x - 3}{x - 2}$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Algebraic Expressions — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Only collect **like terms** — $x$ and $x^2$ are different and cannot be combined.
- When expanding, watch negative signs carefully, especially $-3 \times x = -3x$.
- $(x + a)^2$ is **not** $x^2 + a^2$; remember the middle term $2ax$.
- Always factorise **fully** — check for a common factor before anything else.
- For algebraic fractions you can only cancel **factors**, never individual terms.
- When changing the subject, do the same operation to both sides and keep terms with the new subject together.
- Difference of two squares needs a minus sign between two squares — $x^2 + 9$ does not factorise.

---
## What a Linear Equation Is

A linear equation has variables only to the power $1$ (no $x^2$). The goal is to find the value of the unknown that makes both sides equal.

## The Golden Rule

Whatever you do to one side, do to the other. Use **inverse operations** to undo what is being done to the variable, working in reverse BIDMAS order.

## Solving Two-Step Equations

For $3x + 5 = 20$: subtract $5$, then divide by $3$, giving $x = 5$.

## Variables on Both Sides

Collect the variable terms on one side and the numbers on the other. For $5x - 2 = 2x + 7$: subtract $2x$, add $2$, divide by $3$.

## Equations with Brackets

Expand brackets first, then solve as normal. For $4(x - 3) = 8$, expand to $4x - 12 = 8$.

## Equations with Fractions

Multiply every term by the denominator (or the LCM of the denominators) to clear fractions before solving.

## Forming Equations

Many problems require you to translate words into an equation. Define the unknown with a letter, build the equation from the information, then solve and interpret.

## Checking Solutions

Substitute your answer back into the original equation to confirm both sides are equal — a quick, reliable check.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Linear Equations — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Inverse operations:** $+ \leftrightarrow -$ and $\times \leftrightarrow \div$ undo each other.
- **Balance rule:** perform the same operation on both sides.
- **Order:** undo addition/subtraction first, then multiplication/division (reverse BIDMAS).
- **Brackets:** expand before collecting terms.
- **Fractions:** multiply every term by the LCM of denominators to clear them.
- **Variables both sides:** move all variable terms to one side, all constants to the other.
- **Check:** substitute the solution back into the original equation.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Solving an equation with variables on both sides
---
Solve $5x - 2 = 2x + 13$.

**Step 1 — collect the variable terms on one side.**

Subtract $2x$ from both sides: $5x - 2x - 2 = 13$, giving $3x - 2 = 13$.

**Step 2 — move the constant.**

Add $2$ to both sides: $3x = 15$.

**Step 3 — divide to isolate $x$.**

$x = \frac{15}{3} = 5$.

**Step 4 — check.**

LHS: $5(5) - 2 = 23$. RHS: $2(5) + 13 = 23$. ✓

**The solution is $x = 5$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Solving an equation with fractions
---
Solve $\dfrac{x + 1}{2} + \dfrac{x - 1}{3} = 4$.

**Step 1 — multiply every term by the LCM of the denominators (6).**

$6 \times \frac{x+1}{2} + 6 \times \frac{x-1}{3} = 6 \times 4$.

This gives $3(x + 1) + 2(x - 1) = 24$.

**Step 2 — expand the brackets.**

$3x + 3 + 2x - 2 = 24$.

**Step 3 — collect like terms.**

$5x + 1 = 24$.

**Step 4 — solve.**

$5x = 23$, so $x = \frac{23}{5} = 4.6$.

**The solution is $x = 4.6$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Linear Equations — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Always do the same operation to **both** sides — this is the most important rule.
- Clear fractions by multiplying **every** term (including those without a fraction) by the LCM.
- When moving variables across, change the sign as it crosses the equals sign.
- Expand brackets before collecting terms, watching negatives.
- Don't divide too early — collect like terms first.
- Always check your answer by substituting back into the original equation.
- When forming equations from words, clearly define your variable first.

===NOTE===
type: concept
title: Quadratic Equations — Revision Notes
---
## Standard Form

A quadratic equation is $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ where $a \ne 0$. It can have two, one, or no real solutions (roots).

## Solving by Factorising

If the quadratic factorises, write it as a product of two brackets equal to zero. Since a product is zero only when a factor is zero, set each bracket to zero and solve.

For $x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0$: $(x + 2)(x + 3) = 0$, so $x = -2$ or $x = -3$.

## The Quadratic Formula

When factorising is hard or impossible, use:

$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

The **discriminant** $b^2 - 4ac$ tells you the number of roots: positive $\Rightarrow$ two roots, zero $\Rightarrow$ one repeated root, negative $\Rightarrow$ no real roots.

## Completing the Square

Rewrite $x^2 + bx + c$ as $\left(x + \frac{b}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 + c$. This gives the **turning point** and an exact way to solve.

## Solving Harder Quadratics

When $a \ne 1$, factorise by splitting the middle term, or use the formula. Always rearrange to "$= 0$" first.

## Graphs of Quadratics

A quadratic graph is a **parabola**. The roots are where it crosses the $x$-axis; the turning point is the minimum (if $a > 0$) or maximum (if $a < 0$); the $y$-intercept is $c$.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Quadratic Equations — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Standard form:** $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.
- **Quadratic formula:** $x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$.
- **Discriminant:** $b^2 - 4ac$: $>0$ two roots, $=0$ one root, $<0$ no real roots.
- **Completing the square:** $x^2 + bx + c = \left(x + \dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2 - \dfrac{b^2}{4} + c$.
- **Turning point** of $(x + p)^2 + q$ is $(-p,\ q)$.
- **Zero product rule:** if $AB = 0$ then $A = 0$ or $B = 0$.
- **Factorising $x^2+bx+c$:** find two numbers multiplying to $c$, adding to $b$.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Solving by factorising
---
Solve $x^2 - 7x + 12 = 0$.

**Step 1 — find two numbers that multiply to $+12$ and add to $-7$.**

The numbers are $-3$ and $-4$ (since $(-3)(-4) = 12$ and $-3 + (-4) = -7$).

**Step 2 — write in factorised form.**

$(x - 3)(x - 4) = 0$.

**Step 3 — apply the zero product rule.**

$x - 3 = 0$ or $x - 4 = 0$.

**Step 4 — solve each.**

$x = 3$ or $x = 4$.

**The solutions are $x = 3$ and $x = 4$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Using the quadratic formula
---
Solve $2x^2 + 3x - 4 = 0$, giving solutions to $2$ decimal places.

**Step 1 — identify $a$, $b$, $c$.**

$a = 2$, $b = 3$, $c = -4$.

**Step 2 — substitute into the formula.**

$x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{3^2 - 4(2)(-4)}}{2(2)}$.

**Step 3 — evaluate the discriminant.**

$3^2 - 4(2)(-4) = 9 + 32 = 41$.

**Step 4 — compute both roots.**

$x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{41}}{4} = \frac{-3 \pm 6.4031}{4}$.

$x = \frac{3.4031}{4} = 0.85$ or $x = \frac{-9.4031}{4} = -2.35$.

**The solutions are $x = 0.85$ and $x = -2.35$ (2 d.p.).**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Quadratic Equations — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Always rearrange to the form $= 0$ before factorising or using the formula.
- Take great care with signs in $b^2 - 4ac$, especially when $c$ is negative — $-4ac$ becomes positive.
- A quadratic usually has **two** solutions; don't stop at one.
- When the question says "give solutions to 2 d.p." or "exact form", factorising won't work — use the formula or completing the square.
- For completing the square, halve the coefficient of $x$ and remember to subtract the square.
- Check by substituting roots back into the original equation.
- Use brackets when substituting negatives into the formula to avoid sign errors.

===TOPIC=== gcse-simultaneous
===NOTE===
type: concept
title: Simultaneous Equations — Revision Notes
---
## What They Are

Simultaneous equations are two (or more) equations with two (or more) unknowns, solved together to find values satisfying **all** of them. The solution is where the lines (or curves) intersect.

## Elimination Method (Linear)

1. Multiply one or both equations so that one variable has matching coefficients.
2. **Add** the equations if the matching signs differ, or **subtract** if they are the same, to eliminate that variable.
3. Solve for the remaining variable.
4. Substitute back to find the other variable.

## Substitution Method

Rearrange one equation to make a variable the subject, then substitute into the other equation. This is essential for **linear–quadratic** pairs (one straight line, one curve).

## Linear–Quadratic Simultaneous Equations

When one equation is quadratic, use substitution to get a single quadratic, solve it (factorise or formula), then find the matching values. There are usually two pairs of solutions (two intersection points).

## Graphical Interpretation

The solution is the coordinates of the intersection point(s). A linear pair meets at one point; a line and a curve may meet at two points, one point (tangent), or none.

## Checking

Substitute both values into **both** original equations to confirm they work.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Simultaneous Equations — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Elimination:** match one variable's coefficients, then add (different signs) or subtract (same signs).
- **Substitution:** make one variable the subject and substitute into the other equation.
- **Linear–quadratic:** substitute the linear equation into the quadratic to get one quadratic in a single variable.
- Solutions correspond to **intersection points** of the graphs.
- Linear pairs $\Rightarrow$ one solution; linear–quadratic $\Rightarrow$ up to two solution pairs.
- Always **check** by substituting into both original equations.
- "Same Signs Subtract, Different Signs Add" (SSS / DSA) for elimination.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Solving by elimination
---
Solve $3x + 2y = 16$ and $5x - 2y = 8$.

**Step 1 — line up and decide the operation.**

The $y$ coefficients are $+2$ and $-2$ (same value, different signs), so **add** the equations to eliminate $y$.

**Step 2 — add.**

$(3x + 5x) + (2y - 2y) = 16 + 8$, giving $8x = 24$.

**Step 3 — solve for $x$.**

$x = \frac{24}{8} = 3$.

**Step 4 — substitute back to find $y$.**

$3(3) + 2y = 16 \Rightarrow 9 + 2y = 16 \Rightarrow 2y = 7 \Rightarrow y = 3.5$.

**Step 5 — check in the second equation.**

$5(3) - 2(3.5) = 15 - 7 = 8$. ✓

**The solution is $x = 3$, $y = 3.5$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Linear–quadratic by substitution
---
Solve $y = x + 1$ and $x^2 + y^2 = 25$.

**Step 1 — substitute the linear equation into the quadratic.**

Replace $y$ with $x + 1$: $x^2 + (x + 1)^2 = 25$.

**Step 2 — expand and simplify.**

$x^2 + x^2 + 2x + 1 = 25 \Rightarrow 2x^2 + 2x - 24 = 0$.

Divide by $2$: $x^2 + x - 12 = 0$.

**Step 3 — factorise and solve.**

$(x + 4)(x - 3) = 0$, so $x = -4$ or $x = 3$.

**Step 4 — find the matching $y$ values.**

If $x = -4$: $y = -4 + 1 = -3$.

If $x = 3$: $y = 3 + 1 = 4$.

**The solutions are $(-4, -3)$ and $(3, 4)$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Simultaneous Equations — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Use "Same Signs Subtract, Different Signs Add" to decide between adding and subtracting in elimination.
- When subtracting equations, carefully change every sign in the second equation.
- For linear–quadratic pairs, always use **substitution**, not elimination.
- Remember to find **both** unknowns — finding only one loses marks.
- For curves, expect **two** solution pairs and keep $x$ and $y$ correctly paired.
- Substitute the linear equation, not the quadratic, to keep the algebra simpler.
- Always check both values in both original equations.

===NOTE===
type: concept
title: Inequalities — Revision Notes
---
## Inequality Symbols

- $<$ less than, $>$ greater than.
- $\le$ less than or equal to, $\ge$ greater than or equal to.

## Solving Linear Inequalities

Solve almost exactly like equations using inverse operations. **The one special rule:** if you multiply or divide both sides by a **negative** number, **reverse** the inequality sign.

## Representing on a Number Line

- An **open circle** ($\circ$) shows a strict inequality ($<$ or $>$) — the endpoint is not included.
- A **closed circle** ($\bullet$) shows $\le$ or $\ge$ — the endpoint is included.

## Set Notation & Integer Solutions

Some questions ask for **integer** values satisfying the inequality. List the whole numbers in the allowed range, taking care over whether endpoints are included.

## Double (Compound) Inequalities

For $-3 \le 2x + 1 < 7$, perform the same operation on all three parts to isolate $x$.

## Graphing Inequalities in Two Variables

Each inequality (e.g. $y \le 2x + 1$) corresponds to a region. Draw the boundary line — **dashed** for strict ($<$, $>$) and **solid** for inclusive ($\le$, $\ge$) — then shade the required side. The solution is the overlap of all shaded regions.

## Quadratic Inequalities

Solve the related quadratic equation to find critical values, sketch the parabola, then read off where it is above or below zero.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Inequalities — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- Solve like an equation, **but reverse the sign** when multiplying or dividing by a negative.
- **Open circle** $\circ$: strict ($<$, $>$). **Closed circle** $\bullet$: inclusive ($\le$, $\ge$).
- **Compound:** apply the same operation to all three parts of $a < \text{expr} < b$.
- **Two-variable regions:** dashed line for $<$/$>$; solid line for $\le$/$\ge$.
- **Quadratic inequality:** find roots, then $>0$ means outside the roots (for $a>0$), $<0$ means between the roots.
- Integer solutions: list whole numbers carefully, checking endpoint inclusion.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Solving an inequality involving a negative
---
Solve $4 - 3x > 19$.

**Step 1 — subtract 4 from both sides.**

$-3x > 19 - 4$, giving $-3x > 15$.

**Step 2 — divide both sides by $-3$ and reverse the sign.**

Because we divide by a negative, $>$ becomes $<$:

$x < \frac{15}{-3}$, giving $x < -5$.

**Step 3 — interpret.**

All values less than $-5$ satisfy the inequality.

**The solution is $x < -5$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Solving a quadratic inequality
---
Solve $x^2 - x - 6 < 0$.

**Step 1 — solve the related equation $x^2 - x - 6 = 0$.**

Factorise: $(x - 3)(x + 2) = 0$, so the critical values are $x = 3$ and $x = -2$.

**Step 2 — sketch the parabola.**

The graph is a U-shape (positive $x^2$) crossing the $x$-axis at $-2$ and $3$.

**Step 3 — find where the curve is below zero.**

$x^2 - x - 6 < 0$ means the graph is **below** the $x$-axis, which is **between** the roots.

**The solution is $-2 < x < 3$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Inequalities — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- The single most common error: forgetting to **reverse the inequality** when multiplying or dividing by a negative.
- Match the circle type to the symbol: open for strict, closed for inclusive.
- For graphing, dashed lines mean the boundary is **not** included; solid means it is.
- For quadratic inequalities, sketch the parabola — don't guess the direction of the answer.
- "$<0$" with a positive $x^2$ gives the region **between** the roots; "$>0$" gives outside.
- When listing integers, double-check whether each endpoint should be included.
- Keep the variable on the side that keeps its coefficient positive to avoid sign-flips where possible.

===NOTE===
type: concept
title: Sequences — Revision Notes
---
## Types of Sequence

- **Arithmetic (linear):** a constant amount is added or subtracted each time (common difference).
- **Geometric:** each term is multiplied by a constant ratio.
- **Quadratic:** the **second** difference is constant.
- **Special sequences:** square numbers, cube numbers, triangular numbers, Fibonacci (add the two previous terms).

## The $n$th Term of a Linear Sequence

For an arithmetic sequence, the $n$th term is:

$$\text{$n$th term} = dn + (a - d)$$

where $d$ is the common difference and $a$ is the first term. In practice: the coefficient of $n$ is the common difference; adjust the constant to match the first term.

## Using the $n$th Term

- Substitute a value of $n$ to find a term.
- Set the $n$th term equal to a value and solve to check whether a number is in the sequence (it must give a whole number $n$).

## Quadratic Sequences

The $n$th term has the form $an^2 + bn + c$. The coefficient $a$ is half the constant second difference. Subtract $an^2$ from the sequence to reveal a linear sequence for $bn + c$.

## Geometric Sequences

Each term is the previous one multiplied by the common ratio $r$. The $n$th term is $ar^{n-1}$ (the first term $a$ multiplied by $r$ a total of $n-1$ times).
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Sequences — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Arithmetic $n$th term:** $dn + (a - d)$, where $d$ = common difference, $a$ = first term.
- **Quadratic $n$th term:** $an^2 + bn + c$, where $a = \dfrac{\text{second difference}}{2}$.
- **Geometric $n$th term:** $ar^{n-1}$, where $r$ = common ratio.
- **Triangular numbers:** $\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$.
- **Fibonacci-type:** each term = sum of the two previous terms.
- To test membership, set the $n$th term equal to the value and check $n$ is a positive integer.
- Constant first difference $\Rightarrow$ linear; constant second difference $\Rightarrow$ quadratic.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Finding the nth term of a linear sequence
---
Find the $n$th term of $7,\ 11,\ 15,\ 19,\ \dots$ and use it to find the $50$th term.

**Step 1 — find the common difference.**

$11 - 7 = 4$, so $d = 4$. The $n$th term starts with $4n$.

**Step 2 — adjust the constant.**

For $n = 1$: $4(1) = 4$, but the first term is $7$, so add $3$.

$n$th term $= 4n + 3$.

**Step 3 — check with $n = 2$.**

$4(2) + 3 = 11$. ✓

**Step 4 — find the 50th term.**

$4(50) + 3 = 200 + 3 = 203$.

**The $n$th term is $4n + 3$ and the 50th term is $203$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Finding the nth term of a quadratic sequence
---
Find the $n$th term of $3,\ 8,\ 15,\ 24,\ 35,\ \dots$

**Step 1 — find the differences.**

First differences: $5, 7, 9, 11$. Second differences: $2, 2, 2$ (constant), so it is quadratic.

**Step 2 — find the $n^2$ coefficient.**

$a = \frac{\text{second difference}}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1$, so the term includes $n^2$.

**Step 3 — subtract $n^2$ from each term.**

$n^2$ for $n=1,2,3,4,5$ is $1, 4, 9, 16, 25$.

Sequence minus $n^2$: $3-1, 8-4, 15-9, 24-16, 35-25 = 2, 4, 6, 8, 10$.

**Step 4 — find the $n$th term of this linear part.**

$2, 4, 6, 8, 10$ has $n$th term $2n$.

**Combining: the $n$th term is $n^2 + 2n$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Sequences — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- For linear sequences, the coefficient of $n$ is the **common difference** — find it first.
- A common slip: writing $4n + 7$ instead of adjusting properly; always check $n = 1$ gives the first term.
- For quadratic sequences, the $n^2$ coefficient is **half** the second difference, not the second difference itself.
- To test if a number is in a sequence, set the $n$th term equal to it and check $n$ is a **positive whole number**.
- Don't confuse geometric (multiply) with arithmetic (add) sequences.
- Remember the geometric formula uses $r^{n-1}$, not $r^n$.
- Always verify your $n$th term against at least two terms of the sequence.

===NOTE===

title: Straight-Line Graphs — Revision Notes
---
## The Equation of a Straight Line

The standard form is:

$$y = mx + c$$

where $m$ is the **gradient** (steepness) and $c$ is the **$y$-intercept** (where the line crosses the $y$-axis).

## Gradient

The gradient measures change in $y$ per unit change in $x$:

$$m = \frac{\text{change in } y}{\text{change in } x} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

A positive gradient rises left to right; a negative gradient falls. A larger magnitude means steeper.

## Plotting Lines

- Use a table of values, or
- Plot the $y$-intercept $c$, then use the gradient to step to further points.

## Finding the Equation from Two Points

Find the gradient with the formula, then substitute one point into $y = mx + c$ to find $c$.

## Parallel & Perpendicular Lines

- **Parallel lines** have **equal gradients**.
- **Perpendicular lines** have gradients whose product is $-1$: if one gradient is $m$, the other is $-\frac{1}{m}$ (negative reciprocal).

## Midpoint and Length

- **Midpoint** of $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ is the average of the coordinates.
- **Length** uses Pythagoras on the horizontal and vertical distances.

## Rearranging into $y = mx + c$

Equations like $2x + 3y = 6$ should be rearranged to read off the gradient and intercept.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Straight-Line Graphs — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Line equation:** $y = mx + c$ ($m$ = gradient, $c$ = $y$-intercept).
- **Gradient:** $m = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.
- **Parallel:** equal gradients ($m_1 = m_2$).
- **Perpendicular:** $m_1 \times m_2 = -1$, so $m_2 = -\dfrac{1}{m_1}$.
- **Midpoint:** $\left(\dfrac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\ \dfrac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$.
- **Length:** $\sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$.
- Rearrange any linear equation into $y = mx + c$ to read off $m$ and $c$.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Finding the equation of a line through two points
---
Find the equation of the line through $A(1, 4)$ and $B(3, 10)$.

**Step 1 — find the gradient.**

$m = \frac{10 - 4}{3 - 1} = \frac{6}{2} = 3$.

**Step 2 — substitute a point into $y = mx + c$.**

Using $A(1, 4)$: $4 = 3(1) + c$.

**Step 3 — solve for $c$.**

$4 = 3 + c \Rightarrow c = 1$.

**Step 4 — write the equation.**

$y = 3x + 1$.

**Check with $B$:** $3(3) + 1 = 10$. ✓

**The equation is $y = 3x + 1$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Equation of a perpendicular line
---
Find the equation of the line perpendicular to $y = 2x + 5$ that passes through $(4, 3)$.

**Step 1 — find the perpendicular gradient.**

The original gradient is $2$. The perpendicular gradient is the negative reciprocal: $-\frac{1}{2}$.

**Step 2 — substitute the point into $y = mx + c$.**

$3 = -\frac{1}{2}(4) + c$.

**Step 3 — solve for $c$.**

$3 = -2 + c \Rightarrow c = 5$.

**Step 4 — write the equation.**

$y = -\frac{1}{2}x + 5$.

**The equation is $y = -\dfrac{1}{2}x + 5$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Straight-Line Graphs — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Always rearrange to $y = mx + c$ before reading off the gradient and intercept.
- Gradient is change in $y$ **over** change in $x$ — don't invert it.
- Parallel means **same gradient**; perpendicular means **negative reciprocal** (flip and change sign).
- Watch signs: a line falling to the right has a **negative** gradient.
- For the perpendicular gradient of $2$, the answer is $-\frac{1}{2}$, not $-2$.
- When finding $c$, substitute a known point carefully and solve.
- Check your final equation passes through the given point(s).

title: Other Graphs & Functions — Revision Notes
---
## Quadratic Graphs

$y = ax^2 + bx + c$ gives a **parabola**. It opens upward if $a > 0$ (has a minimum) and downward if $a < 0$ (has a maximum). The roots are where $y = 0$; the $y$-intercept is $c$.

## Cubic Graphs

$y = ax^3 + \dots$ has a characteristic curve with up to two turning points. A positive cubic rises overall from bottom-left to top-right.

## Reciprocal Graphs

$y = \frac{k}{x}$ produces a **hyperbola** with two branches. The axes are **asymptotes** — the curve approaches but never touches them.

## Exponential Graphs

$y = k a^x$ grows (or decays) rapidly. The curve passes through $(0, k)$ and has the $x$-axis as an asymptote. Used to model growth and decay.

## Circles

$x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ is a circle centred at the origin with radius $r$.

## Trigonometric Graphs

- $y = \sin x$ and $y = \cos x$ oscillate between $-1$ and $1$ with period $360^\circ$.
- $y = \tan x$ has period $180^\circ$ with asymptotes.

## Recognising and Using Graphs

Identify graph types from their equations and shapes. Use graphs to **estimate solutions** by reading intersection points, and find gradients of curves using tangents. Function notation $f(x)$ describes the output for an input $x$; transformations shift or stretch curves.

title: Other Graphs & Functions — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Quadratic:** $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ — parabola; minimum if $a>0$, maximum if $a<0$.
- **Cubic:** $y = ax^3 + bx^2 + cx + d$ — up to two turning points.
- **Reciprocal:** $y = \dfrac{k}{x}$ — hyperbola with axes as asymptotes.
- **Exponential:** $y = ka^x$ — passes through $(0, k)$; $x$-axis is an asymptote.
- **Circle (origin):** $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$, radius $r$.
- **Trig periods:** $\sin/\cos$ repeat every $360^\circ$; $\tan$ every $180^\circ$.
- **Transformations:** $f(x) + a$ up; $f(x + a)$ left; $-f(x)$ reflect in $x$-axis; $f(-x)$ reflect in $y$-axis.

type: worked_example
title: Sketching a quadratic graph
---
Sketch $y = x^2 - 2x - 3$, showing the intercepts and turning point.

**Step 1 — find the $y$-intercept.**

Set $x = 0$: $y = -3$. The curve crosses the $y$-axis at $(0, -3)$.

**Step 2 — find the $x$-intercepts (roots).**

Set $y = 0$: $x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0$, which factorises to $(x - 3)(x + 1) = 0$.

Roots: $x = 3$ and $x = -1$.

**Step 3 — find the turning point (by symmetry).**

The axis of symmetry is midway between the roots: $x = \frac{3 + (-1)}{2} = 1$.

At $x = 1$: $y = 1 - 2 - 3 = -4$. Turning point $(1, -4)$.

**Step 4 — describe the shape.**

Since $a = 1 > 0$, it is a U-shaped parabola with a minimum at $(1, -4)$.

**The parabola crosses at $(-1, 0)$ and $(3, 0)$, has $y$-intercept $(0,-3)$, and minimum turning point $(1, -4)$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Applying a graph transformation
---
The graph $y = f(x)$ has a maximum point at $(2, 5)$. State the coordinates of the maximum of $y = f(x) - 3$ and of $y = f(x + 1)$.

**Step 1 — analyse $y = f(x) - 3$.**

Subtracting $3$ from the function shifts the whole graph **down** by $3$. The $x$-coordinate is unchanged; the $y$-coordinate decreases by $3$.

New maximum: $(2,\ 5 - 3) = (2, 2)$.

**Step 2 — analyse $y = f(x + 1)$.**

Replacing $x$ with $x + 1$ shifts the graph **left** by $1$ (inside the bracket moves horizontally, opposite to the sign). The $y$-coordinate is unchanged.

New maximum: $(2 - 1,\ 5) = (1, 5)$.

**For $y = f(x) - 3$ the maximum is $(2, 2)$; for $y = f(x + 1)$ the maximum is $(1, 5)$.**
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Other Graphs & Functions — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Learn to recognise each graph shape from its equation — quadratic, cubic, reciprocal, exponential.
- For a parabola, $a > 0$ gives a minimum (U-shape), $a < 0$ gives a maximum (n-shape).
- Reciprocal and exponential graphs have **asymptotes** they never touch — never let the curve cross them.
- Horizontal transformations $f(x + a)$ move the graph **opposite** to the sign (left for $+a$).
- Vertical transformations $f(x) + a$ move the graph in the **same** direction as the sign.
- The axis of symmetry of a quadratic lies midway between its roots — use this for the turning point.
- For exponential growth, the graph never reaches zero — it approaches the $x$-axis.
