## Basic Angle Facts

Angles are measured in degrees. The key facts you build everything from are:

- **Angles on a straight line** add up to $180°$.
- **Angles around a point** add up to $360°$.
- **Vertically opposite angles** (formed when two straight lines cross) are equal.
- Angles in a **triangle** sum to $180°$; angles in a **quadrilateral** sum to $360°$.

## Angles in Parallel Lines

When a straight line (a **transversal**) crosses two parallel lines:

- **Corresponding angles** are equal (they form an "F" shape).
- **Alternate angles** are equal (they form a "Z" shape).
- **Co-interior** (allied) angles add up to $180°$ (they form a "C" or "U" shape).

Always give a reason when stating these — the exam demands it.

## Interior and Exterior Angles of Polygons

A **polygon** is a closed 2D shape with straight sides. A **regular** polygon has all sides and all angles equal.

- The **sum of interior angles** of an $n$-sided polygon is $(n-2) \times 180°$.
- The **sum of exterior angles** of any polygon is always $360°$.
- At each vertex, **interior angle + exterior angle = $180°$**.

For a **regular** polygon with $n$ sides:

$$
\text{exterior angle} = \frac{360°}{n}, \qquad \text{interior angle} = 180° - \frac{360°}{n}
$$

To find the number of sides from an exterior angle, use $n = \dfrac{360°}{\text{exterior angle}}$.

## Tessellation and Tiling

Shapes **tessellate** if copies fit together with no gaps or overlaps. This happens when angles meeting at a point sum to $360°$. Regular polygons tessellate only when the interior angle divides exactly into $360°$ (equilateral triangle, square, regular hexagon).

## Working with Reasons

Many marks come from **justifying** each step. Acceptable reasons include "angles on a straight line", "alternate angles are equal", and "base angles of an isosceles triangle are equal". Build your answer one logical step at a time.

- Angles around a point sum to $360°$.

===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Finding an interior angle of a regular polygon
---
**Find the size of each interior angle of a regular decagon (10 sides).**

Step 1 — Find the exterior angle. The exterior angles of any polygon sum to $360°$, and they are all equal in a regular polygon:
$$
\text{exterior angle} = \frac{360°}{10} = 36°
$$

Step 2 — Use the fact that interior and exterior angles lie on a straight line:
$$
\text{interior angle} = 180° - 36° = 144°
$$

Check (optional): sum of interior angles $=(10-2)\times 180° = 1440°$, and $1440° \div 10 = 144°$. ✓

Each interior angle is **$144°$**.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Angles in parallel lines with reasons
---
**Two parallel lines are crossed by a transversal. One angle is $112°$. Find angle $x$, the co-interior angle on the same side.**

Step 1 — Identify the relationship. The angle of $112°$ and angle $x$ are between the parallel lines on the same side of the transversal, so they are **co-interior (allied) angles**.

Step 2 — Co-interior angles add up to $180°$:
$$
x + 112° = 180°
$$

Step 3 — Solve:
$$
x = 180° - 112° = 68°
$$

Angle $x = $ **$68°$** (co-interior angles sum to $180°$).
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Angles & Polygons — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- **Always give reasons.** "Angle = $50°$" alone may lose the reasoning mark. State the rule used.
- Don't confuse **alternate** ("Z") and **co-interior** ("C") angles — co-interior add to $180°$, they are not equal.
- Use $(n-2)\times 180°$ for the **interior** sum; exterior angles always sum to $360°$ no matter the number of sides.
- For "how many sides" questions, divide $360°$ by the **exterior** angle, not the interior one.
- In isosceles triangles, mark the equal sides and remember the **base angles are equal**.
- Don't assume a diagram is to scale — never measure with a ruler unless told to.
- Show every step. Long angle-chasing problems often carry method marks for intermediate angles.

## Parts of a Circle

- **Radius**: centre to edge. **Diameter**: across through the centre ($= 2r$).
- **Chord**: a line joining two points on the circle.
- **Tangent**: a line touching the circle at exactly one point.
- **Arc**: part of the circumference. **Sector**: a "pizza slice". **Segment**: region cut off by a chord.

## Circumference and Area

$$
C = \pi d = 2\pi r, \qquad A = \pi r^2
$$

## Arcs and Sectors

For a sector with angle $\theta$ at the centre:

$$
\text{arc length} = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r, \qquad \text{sector area} = \frac{\theta}{360}\times \pi r^2
$$

## Circle Theorems

These are heavily tested at higher tier — learn each with its exact wording:

- The angle in a **semicircle** is $90°$ (angle subtended by a diameter).
- The angle at the **centre** is **twice** the angle at the circumference (when both stand on the same arc).
- Angles in the **same segment** (subtended by the same arc) are **equal**.
- **Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral** add up to $180°$.
- A **tangent** meets a radius at $90°$.
- **Two tangents** from an external point are **equal in length**.
- The **perpendicular from the centre to a chord bisects the chord**.
- **Alternate segment theorem**: the angle between a tangent and a chord equals the angle in the alternate segment.

## Giving Reasons

Each theorem used must be quoted by name. Marks are awarded both for the correct value and for the correct reason, so write them out fully.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Circles — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- Circumference: $C = \pi d = 2\pi r$.
- Area: $A = \pi r^2$.
- Arc length $= \dfrac{\theta}{360}\times 2\pi r$.
- Sector area $= \dfrac{\theta}{360}\times \pi r^2$.
- Angle in a semicircle $= 90°$.
- Angle at centre $= 2\times$ angle at circumference (same arc).
- Angles in the same segment are equal.
- Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral sum to $180°$.
- Tangent $\perp$ radius at the point of contact.
- Two tangents from a point are equal in length.
- Perpendicular from centre bisects a chord.
- Alternate segment theorem: tangent–chord angle $=$ angle in alternate segment.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Using angle at the centre is twice angle at circumference
---
**Points $A$, $B$ and $C$ lie on a circle, centre $O$. The angle $ABC$ at the circumference is $54°$. Find the reflex angle... no — find angle $AOC$ at the centre, standing on the same arc $AC$.**

Step 1 — Identify the theorem. Angle $ABC$ at the circumference and angle $AOC$ at the centre both stand on arc $AC$.

Step 2 — Apply "the angle at the centre is twice the angle at the circumference":
$$
\angle AOC = 2 \times \angle ABC = 2 \times 54°
$$

Step 3 — Calculate:
$$
\angle AOC = 108°
$$

Angle $AOC = $ **$108°$** (angle at centre is twice the angle at the circumference).
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Area of a sector
---
**A sector has radius $8\text{ cm}$ and centre angle $135°$. Find its area, to 1 decimal place.**

Step 1 — Write the sector area formula:
$$
\text{area} = \frac{\theta}{360}\times \pi r^2
$$

Step 2 — Substitute $\theta = 135$ and $r = 8$:
$$
\text{area} = \frac{135}{360}\times \pi \times 8^2
$$

Step 3 — Simplify $8^2 = 64$ and $\dfrac{135}{360} = 0.375$:
$$
\text{area} = 0.375 \times \pi \times 64 = 24\pi
$$

Step 4 — Evaluate:
$$
24\pi = 75.398\ldots
$$

Area $= $ **$75.4\text{ cm}^2$** (1 d.p.).
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Circles — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Memorise all circle theorems **with their reasons** — most marks are split between value and justification.
- Don't mix up $\pi r^2$ (area) and $2\pi r$ (circumference). A common slip is using the diameter in $\pi r^2$.
- For sectors/arcs, keep the fraction $\dfrac{\theta}{360}$ and only round at the very end.
- When a question says "leave your answer in terms of $\pi$", do **not** type it into the calculator.
- Look for the **diameter** — it signals "angle in a semicircle $=90°$".
- A radius drawn to a tangent makes a right angle — mark it before doing trig or Pythagoras.
- Check whether you need the **minor** or **major** sector/arc; subtract from the whole if needed.

## The Four Transformations

A transformation maps an **object** onto an **image**. You must be able to perform and fully describe each.

### Translation
Moves a shape without rotating or reflecting it. Described by a **column vector** $\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$, where $x$ is movement right (negative = left) and $y$ is movement up (negative = down). The shape stays **congruent**.

### Reflection
Flips a shape over a **mirror line**. To describe fully, state the **equation of the line** (e.g. $y = x$, $x = 2$, the $y$-axis). Each point and its image are equidistant from the line. Congruent.

### Rotation
Turns a shape about a fixed **centre of rotation** through an **angle** in a given **direction** (clockwise/anticlockwise). To describe: state the **angle, direction and centre**. Use tracing paper to perform it. Congruent.

### Enlargement
Changes size by a **scale factor (SF)** from a **centre of enlargement**.
- SF $> 1$: bigger. SF between $0$ and $1$: smaller. **Negative** SF: image on the opposite side of the centre and inverted.
- To describe: state the **scale factor and the centre of enlargement**.
- Enlargement is **not** congruent (unless SF $= \pm 1$) but the image is **similar** to the object.

## Combining Transformations

Two transformations performed one after the other can sometimes be described as a **single** transformation. Apply them in order, then describe the overall effect.

## Congruence vs Similarity

- **Translations, reflections and rotations** preserve size — image is **congruent**.
- **Enlargements** preserve shape but change size — image is **similar**.

## Invariance

A point is **invariant** if it does not move under the transformation (e.g. a point on the mirror line, or the centre of rotation/enlargement).
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Transformations — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- **Translation**: column vector $\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$ ($x$ right, $y$ up).
- **Reflection**: describe by the mirror line's equation.
- **Rotation**: describe by angle, direction, centre.
- **Enlargement**: describe by scale factor and centre.
- Length scale factor $k$ ⟹ area scale factor $k^2$.
- Negative enlargement: image inverted and on the opposite side of the centre.
- Translation, reflection, rotation ⟹ **congruent** image.
- Enlargement ⟹ **similar** image.
- A single transformation should be described with **one** transformation only (never two).
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Describing a rotation fully
---
**A triangle has vertices $A(1,1)$, $B(1,3)$, $C(2,1)$. Its image $A'(-1,1)$, $B'(-3,1)$, $C'(-1,2)$ is a rotation. Describe it fully.**

Step 1 — Note size is unchanged, so it is a rotation (not enlargement).

Step 2 — Find the angle. Vertex $A(1,1)$ maps to $A'(-1,1)$. Testing a $90°$ rotation **anticlockwise about the origin**, the rule is $(x,y)\mapsto(-y,x)$. Then $A(1,1)\mapsto(-1,1)$ ✓.

Step 3 — Check another point: $B(1,3)\mapsto(-3,1)$ ✓ and $C(2,1)\mapsto(-1,2)$ ✓.

Step 4 — State all three required parts: angle, direction, centre.

It is a rotation of **$90°$ anticlockwise about the origin $(0,0)$**.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Enlargement with a negative scale factor
---
**Enlarge triangle $P(2,2)$, $Q(4,2)$, $R(2,3)$ by scale factor $-2$ about the centre $(1,1)$. Find the image of $P$.**

Step 1 — Find the vector from the centre $C(1,1)$ to point $P(2,2)$:
$$
\vec{CP} = \begin{pmatrix} 2-1 \\ 2-1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}
$$

Step 2 — Multiply by the scale factor $-2$:
$$
-2 \times \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}
$$

Step 3 — Add this to the centre to find the image $P'$:
$$
P' = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}
$$

The image of $P$ is **$(-1,-1)$**. (The negative SF places it on the opposite side of the centre.)
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Transformations — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- When asked to **describe**, give **every** required detail and use only **one** transformation. Two transformations = no marks.
- For rotations, **use tracing paper** — fix your pencil on the centre and turn.
- Column vector order is $\begin{pmatrix}\text{across} \\ \text{up}\end{pmatrix}$ — don't swap them. Left and down are negative.
- For negative enlargements, the image is **inverted** and on the **other side** of the centre — a very common error.
- Always label the image with dashes ($A'$, $B'$) so you don't lose track.
- Use the words "congruent" (same size) and "similar" (same shape, different size) precisely.
- Find the centre of enlargement by drawing rays through corresponding points until they cross.

## Area of 2D Shapes

- **Rectangle**: $A = \text{length}\times\text{width}$.
- **Triangle**: $A = \tfrac{1}{2}\times \text{base}\times\text{height}$.
- **Parallelogram**: $A = \text{base}\times \text{height}$ (perpendicular height).
- **Trapezium**: $A = \tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h$, where $a$ and $b$ are the parallel sides.
- **Circle**: $A = \pi r^2$.

Find areas of **compound shapes** by splitting them into known shapes and adding, or subtracting a cut-out.

## Volume of Prisms and Cylinders

A **prism** has the same cross-section throughout:
$$
\text{Volume} = \text{area of cross-section} \times \text{length}
$$

- **Cylinder**: $V = \pi r^2 h$.
- **Cuboid**: $V = l\times w\times h$.

## Volume of Pyramids, Cones and Spheres

$$
\text{Cone: } V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h, \qquad \text{Sphere: } V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3
$$

A pyramid has volume $\tfrac{1}{3}\times\text{base area}\times\text{height}$.

## Surface Area

Add up the area of **every face**. For curved surfaces:
- **Cylinder** curved surface $= 2\pi r h$; total $= 2\pi r h + 2\pi r^2$.
- **Cone** curved surface $= \pi r l$ (where $l$ is the slant height); total $=\pi r l + \pi r^2$.
- **Sphere** surface $= 4\pi r^2$.

## Units

Length: cm. Area: cm$^2$. Volume: cm$^3$. To convert, remember $1\text{ cm} = 10\text{ mm}$, so $1\text{ cm}^2 = 100\text{ mm}^2$ and $1\text{ cm}^3 = 1000\text{ mm}^3$. Also $1\text{ litre} = 1000\text{ cm}^3$.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Area & Volume — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- Triangle area: $\tfrac{1}{2}bh$.
- Parallelogram: $bh$. Trapezium: $\tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h$.
- Circle area: $\pi r^2$; circumference: $2\pi r$.
- Prism volume: cross-section area $\times$ length.
- Cylinder: $V=\pi r^2 h$; curved surface $=2\pi r h$.
- Cone: $V=\tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$; curved surface $=\pi r l$.
- Sphere: $V=\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3$; surface area $=4\pi r^2$.
- Pyramid: $V=\tfrac{1}{3}\times\text{base area}\times h$.
- $1\text{ cm}^2=100\text{ mm}^2$, $1\text{ cm}^3=1000\text{ mm}^3$, $1\text{ litre}=1000\text{ cm}^3$.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Volume of a cylinder
---
**A cylinder has radius $5\text{ cm}$ and height $12\text{ cm}$. Find its volume, to 3 significant figures.**

Step 1 — Write the formula:
$$
V = \pi r^2 h
$$

Step 2 — Substitute $r = 5$, $h = 12$:
$$
V = \pi \times 5^2 \times 12
$$

Step 3 — Work out $5^2 = 25$:
$$
V = \pi \times 25 \times 12 = 300\pi
$$

Step 4 — Evaluate:
$$
300\pi = 942.477\ldots
$$

Volume $= $ **$942\text{ cm}^3$** (3 s.f.).
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Area of a trapezium
---
**A trapezium has parallel sides $7\text{ cm}$ and $11\text{ cm}$, with a perpendicular height of $6\text{ cm}$. Find its area.**

Step 1 — Write the formula:
$$
A = \tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h
$$

Step 2 — Substitute $a = 7$, $b = 11$, $h = 6$:
$$
A = \tfrac{1}{2}(7 + 11)\times 6
$$

Step 3 — Add the parallel sides:
$$
A = \tfrac{1}{2}\times 18 \times 6
$$

Step 4 — Calculate:
$$
A = 9 \times 6 = 54
$$

Area $= $ **$54\text{ cm}^2$**.
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Area & Volume — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Use the **perpendicular** height, not a slanted side, in triangle/parallelogram/trapezium area.
- Don't forget the $\tfrac{1}{3}$ in cone and pyramid volumes, or $\tfrac{4}{3}$ in the sphere.
- For surface area, count **every** face — a common error is missing the two circular ends of a cylinder.
- Watch the **units**: area is squared, volume is cubed. Convert before, not after, if the question mixes units.
- The cone formula uses **slant height** $l$ for curved surface but **vertical height** $h$ for volume — find one from the other with Pythagoras.
- Leave answers in terms of $\pi$ when asked; otherwise round only at the end.
- For compound solids, work out each part separately, then add or subtract.

## The Theorem

Pythagoras' theorem applies to **right-angled triangles** only. If the two shorter sides are $a$ and $b$ and the **hypotenuse** (the longest side, opposite the right angle) is $c$:

$$
a^2 + b^2 = c^2
$$

## Finding the Hypotenuse

When you know the two shorter sides, **add** their squares then square root:
$$
c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}
$$

## Finding a Shorter Side

When you know the hypotenuse and one shorter side, **subtract**:
$$
a = \sqrt{c^2 - b^2}
$$

The key decision is: are you finding the longest side (add) or a shorter side (subtract)?

## Checking for a Right Angle

If $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ holds exactly, the triangle is right-angled. If $a^2+b^2 > c^2$ the angle is acute; if less, obtuse.

## Pythagoras in 3D

For a cuboid with dimensions $a$, $b$, $c$, the **space diagonal** $d$ is:
$$
d = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}
$$

This comes from applying Pythagoras twice — once across the base to get the base diagonal, then up to the opposite corner.

## Distance Between Two Points

On a coordinate grid, the distance between $(x_1,y_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2)$ is found by treating the horizontal and vertical gaps as the two short sides:
$$
d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}
$$

## Surds

Answers are often left in **surd form** (e.g. $\sqrt{13}$) for an exact value. Only round when the question asks for a decimal.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Pythagoras' Theorem — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- Right-angled triangles only: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.
- Hypotenuse (add): $c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$.
- Shorter side (subtract): $a = \sqrt{c^2 - b^2}$.
- The hypotenuse is the longest side, opposite the right angle.
- 3D space diagonal of a cuboid: $d = \sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}$.
- Distance between two points: $d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$.
- Right angle test: $a^2+b^2=c^2$ exactly.
- Leave answers in surd form for exact values.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Finding the hypotenuse
---
**A right-angled triangle has shorter sides $6\text{ cm}$ and $8\text{ cm}$. Find the hypotenuse.**

Step 1 — Identify that we want the longest side, so we **add** the squares:
$$
c^2 = a^2 + b^2
$$

Step 2 — Substitute $a = 6$, $b = 8$:
$$
c^2 = 6^2 + 8^2 = 36 + 64 = 100
$$

Step 3 — Square root:
$$
c = \sqrt{100} = 10
$$

The hypotenuse is **$10\text{ cm}$**.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Finding a shorter side
---
**A right-angled triangle has a hypotenuse of $13\text{ cm}$ and one shorter side of $5\text{ cm}$. Find the other shorter side.**

Step 1 — We want a shorter side, so we **subtract** the squares:
$$
a^2 = c^2 - b^2
$$

Step 2 — Substitute $c = 13$ (hypotenuse), $b = 5$:
$$
a^2 = 13^2 - 5^2 = 169 - 25 = 144
$$

Step 3 — Square root:
$$
a = \sqrt{144} = 12
$$

The other shorter side is **$12\text{ cm}$**.
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Pythagoras' Theorem — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- **Add to find the hypotenuse, subtract to find a shorter side** — getting this backwards is the most common error.
- The hypotenuse is always **opposite the right angle** and the **longest** side. Identify it first.
- Don't forget the final **square root** — squaring is only half the job.
- Only use Pythagoras when there's a **right angle**. No right angle means trig or the cosine rule instead.
- Keep full accuracy in your calculator; round only the final answer.
- For 3D problems, sketch the right-angled triangle you're using inside the solid.
- Recognise common triples (3,4,5 and 5,12,13) to check your work quickly.

## Right-Angled Trigonometry: SOHCAHTOA

Label the sides relative to the angle $\theta$: **opposite** (across from $\theta$), **adjacent** (next to $\theta$), **hypotenuse** (longest, opposite the right angle).

$$
\sin\theta = \frac{O}{H}, \qquad \cos\theta = \frac{A}{H}, \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{O}{A}
$$

- To find a **side**, substitute and rearrange.
- To find an **angle**, use the inverse: $\theta = \sin^{-1}(\ldots)$, $\cos^{-1}(\ldots)$ or $\tan^{-1}(\ldots)$.

## Exact Trig Values

You must know these without a calculator:

- $\sin 30° = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $\cos 60° = \tfrac{1}{2}$, $\tan 45° = 1$.
- $\cos 30° = \tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$, $\sin 60° = \tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$.
- $\sin 45° = \cos 45° = \tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$, $\tan 30° = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt3}$, $\tan 60° = \sqrt3$.
- $\sin 0° = 0$, $\cos 0° = 1$, $\sin 90° = 1$, $\cos 90° = 0$.

## Non-Right-Angled Triangles

Label the angles $A$, $B$, $C$ and the sides opposite them $a$, $b$, $c$.

### Sine Rule
Use when you have a matched **side–angle pair**:
$$
\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}
$$

### Cosine Rule
Use for **SAS** (two sides + included angle) to find a side, or **SSS** (three sides) to find an angle:
$$
a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A
$$

### Area of a Triangle
With two sides and the included angle:
$$
\text{Area} = \tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C
$$

## Choosing a Method

Right angle present → SOHCAHTOA. No right angle: a side–angle pair → sine rule; otherwise → cosine rule.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Trigonometry — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- $\sin\theta=\dfrac{O}{H}$, $\cos\theta=\dfrac{A}{H}$, $\tan\theta=\dfrac{O}{A}$.
- Inverse functions find angles: $\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac{O}{A}\right)$ etc.
- Sine rule: $\dfrac{a}{\sin A}=\dfrac{b}{\sin B}=\dfrac{c}{\sin C}$.
- Cosine rule (side): $a^2=b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A$.
- Cosine rule (angle): $\cos A=\dfrac{b^2+c^2-a^2}{2bc}$.
- Area: $\tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C$.
- Exact: $\sin30°=\cos60°=\tfrac12$, $\tan45°=1$, $\cos30°=\sin60°=\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$, $\sin45°=\cos45°=\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$.
- Right angle ⟹ SOHCAHTOA; side–angle pair ⟹ sine rule; else cosine rule.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Finding a side with SOHCAHTOA
---
**In a right-angled triangle, the angle is $35°$ and the hypotenuse is $10\text{ cm}$. Find the side opposite the angle, to 1 d.p.**

Step 1 — Label: we have the **hypotenuse** ($H=10$) and want the **opposite** ($O$). The pair $O$ and $H$ means use **sine** (SOH).
$$
\sin\theta = \frac{O}{H}
$$

Step 2 — Substitute $\theta = 35°$, $H = 10$:
$$
\sin 35° = \frac{O}{10}
$$

Step 3 — Rearrange:
$$
O = 10 \times \sin 35°
$$

Step 4 — Evaluate ($\sin 35° = 0.5736\ldots$):
$$
O = 10 \times 0.5736\ldots = 5.7357\ldots
$$

The opposite side is **$5.7\text{ cm}$** (1 d.p.).
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Cosine rule to find a side
---
**In triangle $ABC$, $b = 7\text{ cm}$, $c = 9\text{ cm}$, and the included angle $A = 40°$. Find side $a$, to 1 d.p.**

Step 1 — Two sides and the included angle (SAS), so use the **cosine rule**:
$$
a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A
$$

Step 2 — Substitute $b = 7$, $c = 9$, $A = 40°$:
$$
a^2 = 7^2 + 9^2 - 2\times 7\times 9 \times \cos 40°
$$

Step 3 — Work out the terms ($\cos 40° = 0.7660\ldots$):
$$
a^2 = 49 + 81 - 126\times 0.7660\ldots = 130 - 96.53\ldots = 33.47\ldots
$$

Step 4 — Square root:
$$
a = \sqrt{33.47\ldots} = 5.785\ldots
$$

Side $a = $ **$5.8\text{ cm}$** (1 d.p.).

type: tip
title: Trigonometry — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Check your calculator is in **degrees** mode — radians give wrong answers.
- Label $O$, $A$, $H$ relative to the angle **before** choosing sin, cos or tan.
- To find an angle with SOHCAHTOA, use the **inverse** ($\sin^{-1}$ etc.), not the normal function.
- Pick the right tool: right angle → SOHCAHTOA; side–angle pair → sine rule; SAS or SSS → cosine rule.
- In the cosine rule, the side you're finding ($a$) must be **opposite** the given angle ($A$).
- Keep full accuracy through the working; rounding early causes errors.
- Learn the exact values — non-calculator papers test them directly.

## What Is a Vector?

A **vector** has both **magnitude** (size) and **direction**. It is written as a **column vector** $\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$ ($x$ across, $y$ up), in bold ($\mathbf{a}$), or with an arrow $\overrightarrow{AB}$.

$\overrightarrow{AB}$ means "the vector from $A$ to $B$". The reverse, $\overrightarrow{BA} = -\overrightarrow{AB}$ — same length, opposite direction.

## Adding and Subtracting

Add or subtract component by component:
$$
\begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} c \\ d \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a+c \\ b+d \end{pmatrix}
$$

Geometrically, addition follows the **nose-to-tail** rule. To get from $A$ to $C$ via $B$:
$$
\overrightarrow{AC} = \overrightarrow{AB} + \overrightarrow{BC}
$$

## Scalar Multiplication

Multiplying a vector by a number (scalar) scales its length:
$$
3\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}
$$

A negative scalar reverses the direction.

## Parallel Vectors and Collinearity

Two vectors are **parallel** if one is a scalar multiple of the other (e.g. $\mathbf{b} = k\mathbf{a}$). If $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overrightarrow{BC}$ are parallel **and** share point $B$, then $A$, $B$, $C$ are **collinear** (lie on one straight line).

## Magnitude

The length (magnitude) of $\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$ is found with Pythagoras:
$$
\left|\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\right| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}
$$

## Geometric Proofs

Higher-tier questions often use a diagram with $\overrightarrow{OA}=\mathbf{a}$, $\overrightarrow{OB}=\mathbf{b}$, and midpoints. Express each required vector in terms of $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$, then compare to prove lines are parallel or points collinear.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Vectors — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- Column vector: $\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$ ($x$ across, $y$ up).
- $\overrightarrow{BA}=-\overrightarrow{AB}$.
- Addition (nose-to-tail): $\overrightarrow{AC}=\overrightarrow{AB}+\overrightarrow{BC}$.
- Add/subtract componentwise.
- Scalar multiple scales length; negative reverses direction.
- Parallel if $\mathbf{b}=k\mathbf{a}$ for some scalar $k$.
- Parallel + shared point ⟹ collinear.
- Magnitude: $\left|\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\right|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$.
- Midpoint $M$ of $AB$: $\overrightarrow{OM}=\overrightarrow{OA}+\tfrac12\overrightarrow{AB}$.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Adding column vectors and finding magnitude
---
**Given $\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$, find $\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}$ and its magnitude.**

Step 1 — Add componentwise:
$$
\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 + (-1) \\ 4 + 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}
$$

Step 2 — Find the magnitude using Pythagoras:
$$
|\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{2^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{4 + 36} = \sqrt{40}
$$

Step 3 — Simplify the surd:
$$
\sqrt{40} = \sqrt{4\times 10} = 2\sqrt{10}
$$

$\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}$ with magnitude **$2\sqrt{10}$** (≈ $6.32$).
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Vector geometry — finding a path
---
**In triangle $OAB$, $\overrightarrow{OA}=\mathbf{a}$ and $\overrightarrow{OB}=\mathbf{b}$. $M$ is the midpoint of $AB$. Express $\overrightarrow{OM}$ in terms of $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$.**

Step 1 — Find $\overrightarrow{AB}$ by going $A\to O \to B$:
$$
\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{AO} + \overrightarrow{OB} = -\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}
$$

Step 2 — $M$ is the midpoint, so from $A$ travel half of $\overrightarrow{AB}$:
$$
\overrightarrow{AM} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a})
$$

Step 3 — Get to $M$ from $O$ via $A$:
$$
\overrightarrow{OM} = \overrightarrow{OA} + \overrightarrow{AM} = \mathbf{a} + \tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a})
$$

Step 4 — Simplify:
$$
\overrightarrow{OM} = \mathbf{a} + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{b} - \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a} + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{b}
$$

So $\overrightarrow{OM} = $ **$\tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b})$**.
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Vectors — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Watch the **direction**: $\overrightarrow{AB} = -\overrightarrow{BA}$. Reversing a vector flips its sign.
- Build paths **nose-to-tail** — the middle letters should match (e.g. $\overrightarrow{AB}+\overrightarrow{BC}$).
- Keep $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ terms separate and collect like terms carefully in proofs.
- To prove **parallel**, show one vector is a scalar multiple of the other ($k\mathbf{a}$).
- To prove **collinear**, show two vectors are parallel **and** share a common point.
- A clearly written conclusion ("therefore parallel because one is a multiple of the other") earns the final mark.
- Don't confuse the column vector $\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}$ order — $x$ first (across), $y$ second (up).

## Tools and Rules

Constructions must be done with a **pair of compasses and a straight edge (ruler)** only. **Leave all construction arcs visible** — rubbing them out loses marks.

## Standard Constructions

### Perpendicular Bisector of a Line $AB$
Open the compasses to more than half of $AB$. From $A$ draw arcs above and below; repeat from $B$ with the same radius. Join the two crossing points. This line is **perpendicular to $AB$** and passes through its **midpoint**. Every point on it is **equidistant from $A$ and $B$**.

### Angle Bisector
From the vertex, draw an arc cutting both arms. From each of those two points, draw arcs that cross in the middle. Join the vertex to the crossing point. This line cuts the angle exactly in half; every point on it is **equidistant from the two lines**.

### Perpendicular from a Point to a Line
Used to find the shortest distance from a point to a line.

### Constructing $60°$
An equilateral triangle construction gives a $60°$ angle; bisecting it gives $30°$.

## Loci

A **locus** is the set of all points satisfying a rule. The key loci are:

- **Fixed distance from a point** → a **circle** (radius = that distance).
- **Fixed distance from a line** → two **parallel lines** with rounded ends.
- **Equidistant from two points** → the **perpendicular bisector** of the segment joining them.
- **Equidistant from two lines** → the **angle bisector** between them.

## Regions

Many problems ask for a **region** satisfying several conditions (e.g. "within $3\text{ m}$ of $A$ **and** nearer to wall $X$ than wall $Y$"). Draw each locus, then **shade** the overlap. Use a solid line for "up to and including" and watch for "less than" vs "more than".
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Constructions & Loci — Key Facts & Methods
---
- Use **compasses and straight edge only**; leave construction arcs showing.
- **Perpendicular bisector**: equal arcs from each end, join crossings — equidistant from two points.
- **Angle bisector**: arc from vertex, then arcs from those points — equidistant from two lines.
- Locus a fixed distance from a **point** = a circle.
- Locus a fixed distance from a **line** = parallel lines with rounded ends.
- Equidistant from two **points** = perpendicular bisector.
- Equidistant from two **lines** = angle bisector.
- Equilateral triangle construction gives $60°$; bisect for $30°$.
- For regions, draw each locus then shade the overlap.

title: Constructing a perpendicular bisector
---
**Construct the perpendicular bisector of a line segment $AB$ of length $8\text{ cm}$.**

Step 1 — Draw $AB = 8\text{ cm}$ accurately with a ruler.

Step 2 — Set the compasses to a radius **more than half** of $AB$, i.e. more than $4\text{ cm}$ (say $6\text{ cm}$). Keeping this radius fixed for all arcs is essential.

Step 3 — Place the compass point on $A$ and draw an arc above and an arc below the line.

Step 4 — Without changing the radius, place the point on $B$ and draw arcs above and below so they cross the first pair, at points $P$ (above) and $Q$ (below).

Step 5 — Draw a straight line through $P$ and $Q$.

This line is the **perpendicular bisector**: it crosses $AB$ at its midpoint at $90°$, and every point on it is equidistant from $A$ and $B$. **Leave all arcs visible.**

type: worked_example
title: Shading a region satisfying two conditions
---
**A goat is tied in a field. It must stay within $5\text{ m}$ of post $A$ and be nearer to fence $L_1$ than fence $L_2$. Describe how to construct and shade the region.**

Step 1 — "Within $5\text{ m}$ of $A$" is a **circle** of radius $5\text{ m}$ centred on $A$ (using the scale). The region is **inside** this circle.

Step 2 — "Nearer to $L_1$ than $L_2$" is found using the **angle bisector** of the angle between $L_1$ and $L_2$. The required side is the half nearer $L_1$.

Step 3 — Draw both loci accurately, keeping construction arcs.

Step 4 — **Shade the overlap**: the area that is both inside the circle **and** on the $L_1$ side of the bisector.

The valid region is the **overlap of the two conditions** — shade only where both are satisfied.

- **Do not erase your arcs** — they are the evidence the construction is correct, and erasing them loses marks.
- Keep the compass radius **fixed** while drawing a matching pair of arcs.
- Use a sharp pencil and work accurately; constructions are marked on precision (usually within $2\text{ mm}$ and $2°$).
- "Equidistant from two **points**" = perpendicular bisector; "equidistant from two **lines**" = angle bisector. Don't mix them up.
- Read the scale carefully (e.g. $1\text{ cm} = 2\text{ m}$) and convert real distances before drawing.
- For regions, decide whether the boundary is **included** and **shade the correct side** clearly.
- Don't measure angles with a protractor when asked to **construct** — use compasses.

## Averages and Range

- **Mean**: add all values, divide by how many there are. Uses every value but is affected by outliers.
- **Median**: the middle value when data is put **in order**. For $n$ values it is in position $\tfrac{n+1}{2}$; for an even $n$, average the two middle values. Not affected by outliers.
- **Mode**: the most common value. The only average usable for non-numerical data. There can be no mode or several.
- **Range**: largest − smallest. A measure of **spread**, not an average.

## Mean from a Frequency Table

Multiply each value $x$ by its frequency $f$, total these, then divide by the total frequency:
$$
\text{mean} = \frac{\sum fx}{\sum f}
$$

## Grouped Data

With grouped data you use the **midpoint** of each class as $x$. The result is an **estimate** of the mean (you've lost the exact values). The **modal class** is the group with the highest frequency. The class containing the median is found using the $\tfrac{n+1}{2}$ position.

## Quartiles and the Interquartile Range

When data is ordered:

- **Lower quartile (LQ, $Q_1$)**: value $\tfrac{1}{4}$ of the way through.
- **Median ($Q_2$)**: halfway.
- **Upper quartile (UQ, $Q_3$)**: $\tfrac{3}{4}$ of the way through.

$$
\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1
$$

The IQR measures the spread of the **middle 50%** and ignores extreme values, so it is more robust than the range.

## Comparing Distributions

Always compare **one average and one measure of spread**, in context. A smaller IQR/range means more **consistent** data.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Statistical Measures — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- Mean $=\dfrac{\text{sum of values}}{\text{number of values}}$.
- Mean from table $=\dfrac{\sum fx}{\sum f}$.
- Median position: $\tfrac{n+1}{2}$ th value (when ordered).
- Mode: most frequent value (modal class for grouped data).
- Range $=$ largest $-$ smallest.
- Grouped mean uses class **midpoints** and gives an **estimate**.
- Quartiles: $Q_1$ at $\tfrac{1}{4}$, $Q_3$ at $\tfrac{3}{4}$ of the ordered data.
- Interquartile range $\text{IQR}=Q_3-Q_1$.
- Compare distributions using one average **and** one spread, in context.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Median, range and IQR from a list
---
**Find the median, range and interquartile range of: $4, 7, 8, 11, 12, 15, 19, 21, 25$.**

Step 1 — The data is already in order. Count: $n = 9$ values.

Step 2 — Median position $=\tfrac{n+1}{2} = \tfrac{9+1}{2} = 5$th value:
$$
\text{median} = 12
$$

Step 3 — Range $=$ largest $-$ smallest $= 25 - 4 = 21$.

Step 4 — Lower quartile: median of the lower half $4, 7, 8, 11$, so $Q_1 = \tfrac{7+8}{2} = 7.5$.

Step 5 — Upper quartile: median of the upper half $15, 19, 21, 25$, so $Q_3 = \tfrac{19+21}{2} = 20$.

Step 6 — IQR $= Q_3 - Q_1 = 20 - 7.5 = 12.5$.

**Median $= 12$, range $= 21$, IQR $= 12.5$.**
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Estimated mean from a grouped frequency table
---
**Estimate the mean of: $0<t\le 10$ (freq 3), $10<t\le 20$ (freq 7), $20<t\le 30$ (freq 5).**

Step 1 — Find the **midpoint** $x$ of each class: $5$, $15$, $25$.

Step 2 — Multiply each midpoint by its frequency $f$ to get $fx$:
$$
3\times 5 = 15, \quad 7\times 15 = 105, \quad 5\times 25 = 125
$$

Step 3 — Total the frequencies and the $fx$ column:
$$
\sum f = 3+7+5 = 15, \qquad \sum fx = 15 + 105 + 125 = 245
$$

Step 4 — Divide:
$$
\text{mean} = \frac{\sum fx}{\sum f} = \frac{245}{15} = 16.33\ldots
$$

Estimated mean $= $ **$16.3$** (1 d.p.).

type: tip
title: Statistical Measures — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- **Order the data first** before finding the median or quartiles — a classic slip.
- The grouped mean is an **estimate** — say so, and use **midpoints**, not class widths.
- Don't confuse **frequency** (how many) with the **value** itself when reading tables.
- For comparisons, write a sentence comparing an **average** and the **spread** in context — both are needed for full marks.
- Range and IQR are measures of **spread**, not averages — don't list them as an "average".
- A smaller IQR/range means **more consistent** data; say which dataset and why.
- Remember the median position is $\tfrac{n+1}{2}$ — for $n=20$ that's the $10.5$th, i.e. midway between the 10th and 11th values.

## Reading and Drawing Common Charts

- **Bar charts**: bars of equal width; height shows frequency. Leave gaps between bars for discrete/categorical data.
- **Pictograms**: a symbol represents a set number; read the **key** carefully.
- **Pie charts**: the whole circle ($360°$) represents the total. Each category's angle is $\dfrac{\text{frequency}}{\text{total}}\times 360°$.

## Frequency Polygons

Plot the **frequency against the midpoint** of each class and join the points with straight lines. Used to show the shape of grouped data and to compare distributions.

## Histograms (with Unequal Class Widths)

When classes have **different widths**, use a histogram with **frequency density** on the vertical axis:
$$
\text{frequency density} = \frac{\text{frequency}}{\text{class width}}
$$

Crucially, the **area** of each bar (not its height) represents the frequency. To read a frequency back:
$$
\text{frequency} = \text{frequency density} \times \text{class width}
$$

## Cumulative Frequency

A **running total** of frequencies. Plot cumulative frequency against the **upper class boundary** and join with a smooth curve. From the curve you can read:

- The **median** (at $\tfrac{n}{2}$ up the cumulative axis),
- The **quartiles** ($\tfrac{n}{4}$ and $\tfrac{3n}{4}$),
- The number of values above/below a given amount.

## Box Plots

A box plot shows five values: minimum, $Q_1$, median, $Q_3$, maximum. The box spans the IQR. Box plots make comparing two distributions quick — compare medians and the spread (IQR).

## Time Series

A line graph over time, useful for spotting **trends** and seasonal patterns.

type: formula
title: Charts & Diagrams — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- Pie chart angle $=\dfrac{\text{frequency}}{\text{total}}\times 360°$.
- Frequency polygon: plot frequency vs class **midpoint**.
- Frequency density $=\dfrac{\text{frequency}}{\text{class width}}$.
- Histogram: **area** of bar $=$ frequency; frequency $=$ density $\times$ width.
- Cumulative frequency: running total, plotted at the **upper class boundary**.
- From a CF curve: median at $\tfrac{n}{2}$, $Q_1$ at $\tfrac{n}{4}$, $Q_3$ at $\tfrac{3n}{4}$.
- Box plot shows: min, $Q_1$, median, $Q_3$, max; box width $=$ IQR.
- Compare distributions with median and IQR.

**In a survey of $40$ people, $14$ chose tea. Find the angle for "tea" on a pie chart.**

Step 2 — Substitute frequency $= 14$, total $= 40$:

## Basic Probability

Probability measures how likely an event is, on a scale from $0$ (impossible) to $1$ (certain). For equally likely outcomes:
$$
P(\text{event}) = \frac{\text{number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{total number of outcomes}}
$$

The probabilities of all possible outcomes **add up to $1$**, so:
$$
P(\text{not } A) = 1 - P(A)
$$

## Experimental Probability

From data, the **relative frequency** estimates probability:
$$
\text{relative frequency} = \frac{\text{number of successes}}{\text{number of trials}}
$$

More trials give a more reliable estimate. **Expected frequency** $=$ probability $\times$ number of trials.

## Combined Events

- **Mutually exclusive** events (can't both happen): $P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)$.
- **Independent** events (one doesn't affect the other): $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A)\times P(B)$.

## Tree Diagrams

Each branch shows an outcome and its probability; branches from a point sum to $1$. **Multiply along** branches to find combined probabilities, then **add** the relevant final outcomes.

## With and Without Replacement

- **With replacement**: probabilities stay the same on the second pick.
- **Without replacement**: the total decreases by 1, and the count of the chosen colour decreases — so the second-pick probabilities **change**. This is **conditional** probability.

## Venn Diagrams and Set Notation

- $A \cap B$ = intersection ("and", in both).
- $A \cup B$ = union ("or", in either).
- $A'$ = complement ("not $A$").
Use Venn diagrams to organise overlapping groups and read off probabilities.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Probability — Key Facts & Formulas
---
- $P(\text{event})=\dfrac{\text{favourable outcomes}}{\text{total outcomes}}$.
- All outcome probabilities sum to $1$; $P(\text{not }A)=1-P(A)$.
- Relative frequency $=\dfrac{\text{successes}}{\text{trials}}$.
- Expected frequency $=$ probability $\times$ number of trials.
- Mutually exclusive: $P(A\text{ or }B)=P(A)+P(B)$.
- Independent: $P(A\text{ and }B)=P(A)\times P(B)$.
- Tree diagrams: **multiply** along branches, **add** outcomes.
- Without replacement: totals change (conditional probability).
- Sets: $\cap$ = and/intersection, $\cup$ = or/union, $A'$ = not $A$.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Tree diagram without replacement
---
**A bag has $5$ red and $3$ blue counters. Two are drawn without replacement. Find the probability both are red.**

Step 1 — First counter: $P(\text{red}) = \dfrac{5}{8}$ (5 red out of 8).

Step 2 — Without replacement, one red is gone: $7$ counters left, $4$ red:
$$
P(\text{second red}) = \frac{4}{7}
$$

Step 3 — Multiply along the branches for "red **and** red":
$$
P(\text{both red}) = \frac{5}{8}\times \frac{4}{7} = \frac{20}{56}
$$

Step 4 — Simplify:
$$
\frac{20}{56} = \frac{5}{14}
$$

$P(\text{both red}) = $ **$\dfrac{5}{14}$**.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Expected frequency
---
**A biased dice has $P(\text{six}) = 0.3$. It is rolled $200$ times. How many sixes are expected?**

Step 1 — Use expected frequency $=$ probability $\times$ number of trials:
$$
\text{expected sixes} = P(\text{six})\times \text{number of rolls}
$$

Step 2 — Substitute $P(\text{six}) = 0.3$ and $200$ rolls:
$$
\text{expected sixes} = 0.3 \times 200
$$

Step 3 — Calculate:
$$
0.3 \times 200 = 60
$$

We expect about **$60$ sixes**. (This is an estimate — the actual number will vary.)
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Probability — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Probabilities are always between $0$ and $1$ — never write a value above $1$ or a percentage in a sum that needs a fraction.
- Use $P(\text{not }A)=1-P(A)$ to save work.
- On tree diagrams, **multiply along** branches and **add** the separate outcomes.
- **Without replacement**: reduce both the chosen count **and** the total for the second pick — forgetting this is the top error.
- "At least one" is often easiest via $1 - P(\text{none})$.
- Keep answers as **fractions** unless told otherwise; don't round probabilities early.
- In Venn diagrams, fill the **intersection first**, then work outwards.

## What a Scatter Graph Shows

A **scatter graph** plots paired data for two variables (e.g. height vs weight) to see if they are **related**. Each point is one observation.

## Correlation

**Correlation** describes the relationship between the two variables:

- **Positive correlation**: as one variable increases, so does the other (points slope upwards).
- **Negative correlation**: as one increases, the other decreases (points slope downwards).
- **No correlation**: no clear pattern; points are scattered randomly.

You can also describe the **strength**: **strong** (points close to a line) or **weak** (more spread out).

## Correlation Is Not Causation

A correlation does **not** prove that one variable **causes** the other to change — there may be another factor involved. Always be cautious about claiming cause.

## Line of Best Fit

A **line of best fit** is a single straight line drawn through the data to show the trend. Rules:

- It should follow the general direction of the points.
- Roughly **equal numbers of points lie above and below** it.
- It does **not** need to pass through the origin, and shouldn't be forced to touch the first/last point.

## Using the Line of Best Fit

- **Interpolation**: predicting a value **within** the range of the data — this is reliable.
- **Extrapolation**: predicting **outside** the data range — this is unreliable, because the pattern may not continue.

## Outliers

An **outlier** is a point that does not fit the pattern. It might be a measurement or recording error. Ignore outliers when drawing the line of best fit, but mention them if asked.
===NOTE===
type: formula
title: Scatter Graphs — Key Facts & Methods
---
- Scatter graphs show the relationship between **two** variables.
- Positive correlation: both increase together (upward slope).
- Negative correlation: one increases as the other decreases (downward slope).
- No correlation: no pattern.
- Strength: strong (close to a line) or weak (spread out).
- **Correlation does not imply causation.**
- Line of best fit: follows the trend with roughly equal points either side.
- **Interpolation** (within data) is reliable; **extrapolation** (outside) is not.
- An **outlier** is a point that doesn't fit the pattern; exclude it from the line of best fit.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Using a line of best fit to estimate
---
**A scatter graph of revision hours ($x$) against test score ($y$) shows strong positive correlation. The line of best fit passes through $(2, 40)$ and $(8, 76)$. Estimate the score for a student who revised $5$ hours.**

Step 1 — Find the gradient of the line of best fit:
$$
\text{gradient} = \frac{76 - 40}{8 - 2} = \frac{36}{6} = 6
$$

Step 2 — Find the equation using $y = mx + c$ with the point $(2, 40)$:
$$
40 = 6\times 2 + c \implies 40 = 12 + c \implies c = 28
$$

So $y = 6x + 28$.

Step 3 — Substitute $x = 5$ (this is **interpolation**, within the data range, so it's reliable):
$$
y = 6\times 5 + 28 = 30 + 28 = 58
$$

The estimated score is about **$58$**.
===NOTE===
type: worked_example
title: Describing correlation and reliability
---
**A scatter graph plots ice-cream sales against daily temperature. The points rise steeply from bottom-left to top-right, lying close to a straight line. Describe the correlation and comment on predicting sales at $45°\text{C}$, given the data only goes up to $30°\text{C}$.**

Step 1 — The points rise from left to right, so as temperature increases, sales increase: this is **positive correlation**.

Step 2 — The points lie **close** to a straight line, so the correlation is **strong**.

Step 3 — Predicting sales at $45°\text{C}$ is **extrapolation**, because $45°\text{C}$ is **outside** the data range (which stops at $30°\text{C}$).

Step 4 — Conclusion: the prediction is **unreliable**, since we cannot assume the pattern continues beyond the observed data.

The relationship is **strong positive correlation**, and a prediction at $45°\text{C}$ would be **unreliable (extrapolation)**.
===NOTE===
type: tip
title: Scatter Graphs — Exam Tips & Common Mistakes
---
- Describe correlation with **both** type (positive/negative/none) **and** strength (strong/weak).
- Never claim one variable **causes** the other — correlation is not causation.
- Draw the line of best fit with a ruler so roughly **equal points** lie each side; don't force it through the origin.
- Use the line to **read off** predictions — go up/across from the axis to the line.
- Predicting **inside** the data range (interpolation) is fine; **outside** (extrapolation) is unreliable — say so.
- Ignore obvious **outliers** when drawing the line, but identify them if asked.
- Plot points accurately with small crosses, and read both axes' scales carefully.
